## Mandate

Director of Quant / ML Trading at Oxford Algorithms, a systematic
fund. Responsible for the applied-ML side of the research operation:
alpha models, signal robustness, and the pipeline that turns a
promising research artefact into a strategy actually running against
the P&L.

## What the research looked like

Financial time series are the canonical case for the hard version of
machine learning: **high-dimensional inputs, low signal-to-noise, and
non-stationary in every sense that matters** — regime changes, changing
liquidity, changing microstructure, drift in the joint distribution of
almost everything you might condition on.

We leaned on three families of methods, chosen for the noise / stability
tradeoff more than fashion:

- **Ensemble learners** (gradient boosting, random forests) as the
  base rate — they handle noisy tabular features gracefully and their
  failure modes are well-understood.
- **Bayesian techniques** where uncertainty quantification mattered
  more than raw predictive accuracy — position-sizing, risk overlays,
  regime detection.
- **Regularised regression** as the honest baseline: when a
  fancy model can't beat properly-regularised ridge on out-of-sample,
  the fancy model isn't real. Half the time it can't.

## Robustness as a first-class problem

More time went into signal *robustness* than into signal *discovery*.
A strategy that works on a 2019–2022 backtest and stops working in
2023 didn't have an alpha; it had a regime alignment. Standard tools
we used continuously:

- **Walk-forward re-fitting** on rolling windows, comparing
  in-sample-cheat vs out-of-sample performance to catch leakage.
- **Regime-conditional performance decompositions** — a model that
  produces most of its P&L in one regime slice is a model that will
  disappoint you when that regime ends.
- **Stress on the joint distribution of inputs**, not just marginals.
  If the correlation structure between your features and the label
  drifts, the model breaks in ways univariate stability plots don't
  catch.

## Pipeline discipline

The research → production pipeline is where most quant shops leak
alpha, not in the model itself. What we insisted on:

- **One canonical data path.** Same feature construction from research
  through production. If you can't reproduce a research backtest bit-for-bit
  from live-shaped data, you're going to be surprised in live.
- **Every model gets a validation harness before deployment.** Not
  "does the notebook print a good number", but a scripted suite that
  runs against held-out data and produces a pass/fail with reasons.
- **PostgreSQL as the backbone** for trades, market data, model outputs,
  and metrics — one queryable source of truth for research and
  post-trade analysis alike.

## What transferred forward

Two years of running the full research-to-production cycle at Oxford
was the direct precursor to how I now run Hercle — the same shape,
just faster and with more of the SDLC automated.
