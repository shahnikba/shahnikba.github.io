A deep-learning system that converts images of Persian text into
machine-readable characters. Handles both **printed** documents
(scans, photos of books) and **handwritten** text.

## Why Persian is harder than Latin OCR

Farsi is written in a modified Arabic script with characteristics
that break most off-the-shelf OCR pipelines:

- **Cursive by default.** Adjacent letters connect, so segmenting a
  word into characters isn't obvious — the same glyph can have up to
  four positional forms (isolated, initial, medial, final).
- **Right-to-left** reading order, mixed with left-to-right numerals
  and any embedded Latin text.
- **Optional diacritics** (short-vowel marks). Usually omitted, but
  when present they change meaning.
- **Highly variable handwriting styles** — Nastaʿlīq, Naskh,
  Shekasteh each have very different letter shapes.

## Architecture

- **Text detection** — locate text regions in the image (rotated
  bounding boxes for scanned or photographed pages that aren't
  perfectly aligned).
- **Line and word segmentation** — extract lines and then words,
  respecting the RTL flow.
- **Sequence recognition** — a CNN + BiLSTM + CTC pipeline maps
  variable-length image strips directly to character sequences
  without needing per-character bounding boxes.
- **Language-model rescoring** — a Persian character-level LM
  disambiguates visually similar options (e.g. dotted vs. undotted
  variants of the same base shape).

## Data

Combined printed corpora (photocopied books, magazine scans) with
handwritten samples. Augmented aggressively — rotations, elastic
distortions, ink-density variations, background noise — because raw
handwritten data is scarce.
