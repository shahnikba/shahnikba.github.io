Full-batch gradient descent uses the exact gradient of the average
loss. **Stochastic gradient descent** uses an unbiased noisy
estimate, computed on a mini-batch of examples, and updates parameters
before finishing the epoch.

The trade looks like a compromise. It's often better than the
non-compromise.

## The update

For a training loss $f(\theta) = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N} f_i(\theta)$
and a mini-batch $B_t \subset \{1, \ldots, N\}$ of size $|B_t| = b$
sampled i.i.d.:

$$
\theta_{t+1} \;=\; \theta_t - \eta_t \cdot \frac{1}{b} \sum_{i \in B_t} \nabla f_i(\theta_t).
$$

The batch-averaged gradient is an unbiased estimator of the true
gradient with variance $\sigma^2 / b$.

## Why the noise doesn't kill it

Naïvely you'd expect the noise to prevent convergence. And it does
prevent convergence to a point — SGD with a fixed step size oscillates
in a neighbourhood of the minimum whose diameter scales with $\eta
\sigma$. That's why you need a decreasing step-size schedule if you
want asymptotic convergence.

The Robbins–Monro conditions give the sufficient recipe:

$$
\sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \eta_t = \infty, \qquad \sum_{t=1}^{\infty} \eta_t^{2} < \infty.
$$

Any schedule that decays slower than $1/t$ but faster than $1/\sqrt{t}$
works. In deep learning practice, $\eta_t$ is usually a step-wise
decay: constant for a while, then divided by 10 at milestones. It's
not elegant but it works.

## The variance-vs-cost trade

Doubling the batch size halves the gradient variance but doubles the
per-step compute. At fixed total compute budget, larger batches let
you take fewer, higher-quality steps; smaller batches let you take
more, noisier steps.

In practice the trade is not symmetric:

- **Very small batches** (e.g., $b=1$, true SGD) are noisy enough that
  you're mostly wandering. Convergence is slow per step and per
  wall-clock.
- **Very large batches** (approaching full-batch) can *generalise*
  worse in neural networks. The "large-batch generalisation gap" is
  well-documented empirically; the explanation is a live research
  area.
- **Moderate batches** (typically 32–1024 for deep learning; smaller
  for smaller models) are the sweet spot.

## Why SGD generalises better than GD

This is the surprising empirical finding. On overparameterised neural
networks, SGD systematically finds solutions that generalise better
than full-batch gradient descent — even when both reach the same
training loss.

The prevailing explanation: SGD's noise acts as an *implicit
regulariser*, biasing the optimiser toward flat minima. Flat minima
are more robust to parameter perturbations, which correlates with
better test-set performance. Sharp minima found by full-batch
methods fit the training data as well but generalise worse.

You can view SGD as approximately solving a modified objective that
penalises curvature — the effective regularisation strength scales
with $\eta / b$. This is why larger batch sizes require careful
learning-rate tuning to recover the generalisation of smaller
batches.

## Practical checklist for SGD in the wild

- **Shuffle** the data at the start of each epoch. Without shuffling,
  SGD's noise becomes correlated across steps and the theoretical
  guarantees break.
- **Warm up** the learning rate for the first few hundred steps —
  linearly ramp from zero to the target rate. Prevents early
  divergence when the model is at its most fragile.
- **Decay** the learning rate on plateaus or at pre-set milestones.
- **Momentum** is a near-free upgrade — always on by default in
  practice.

## What replaced it (partially)

The Adam family displaced SGD in many deep-learning workflows
because it's more forgiving on the learning-rate hyperparameter. But
for problems where you can afford to tune, well-configured SGD with
momentum often matches or beats Adam on generalisation — most
famously in ResNet-style vision architectures. Which one wins depends
on the specific setup; both are worth trying.

The underlying point: SGD isn't a compromise on gradient descent.
It's a genuinely different algorithm with an implicit regularisation
that often works in your favour.
