Every applied statistician learns "correlation isn't causation" as a
warning. Causal inference is what happens when you take that warning
seriously and ask *what would be enough*.

## Where regression stops

A textbook regression model $Y = \beta_0 + \beta_1 X + \varepsilon$
identifies the best linear predictor of $Y$ given $X$. It says nothing
— on its own — about what would happen to $Y$ if we *changed* $X$.

That distinction is easy to miss because in a properly randomised
experiment, the two coincide: because $X$ was assigned independently of
everything else, the observed conditional mean $E[Y \mid X]$ equals the
interventional mean $E[Y \mid do(X)]$. But in observational data — the
kind almost everyone actually has — they don't.

## Pearl's three rungs

Judea Pearl organises the whole territory into a ladder with three
rungs, each strictly stronger than the one below:

1. **Association.** $P(y \mid x)$ — what I see about $y$ when I observe
   $x$. Regression, classification, standard supervised learning.
2. **Intervention.** $P(y \mid do(x))$ — what I would see about $y$ if
   I *forced* $X$ to take value $x$, breaking the mechanism that
   normally generates $X$. RCTs, A/B tests, and any properly adjusted
   observational analysis live here.
3. **Counterfactuals.** $P(y_x \mid x', y')$ — what *would have*
   happened to a specific unit if $X$ had been $x$, given that we
   actually observed $X = x'$ and $Y = y'$. The most useful rung for
   post hoc decision analysis, and the hardest to identify.

The rungs are cumulative. You cannot answer a rung-2 question with
rung-1 data alone — you need extra structure (a graph, an experiment,
or explicit assumptions). Rung 3 needs strictly more than rung 2.

## The tool: causal DAGs

The concrete apparatus is a **directed acyclic graph** (DAG) whose
nodes are variables and whose edges point from cause to effect. Given
such a graph, the graph itself tells you what you need to control for
to identify a causal effect — that's what the backdoor and frontdoor
criteria do.

The graph doesn't come from the data. It comes from your subject-matter
understanding of how the variables generate each other. This is the
part that makes some statisticians uncomfortable: causal inference
requires structural assumptions you cannot fully verify. But the
alternative — pretending you can extract causation from correlation
alone — is not neutrality; it's a hidden assumption that all
associations happen to be causal.

## Why regression alone can be misleading

The canonical failure mode is the **confounder**:

$$
Z \rightarrow X, \qquad Z \rightarrow Y
$$

If $Z$ affects both the treatment $X$ and the outcome $Y$, then the
observed correlation between $X$ and $Y$ mixes the causal effect of $X$
on $Y$ with the spurious association induced by $Z$. Regressing $Y$ on
$X$ alone gives you a biased causal estimate. Regressing $Y$ on both
$X$ and $Z$ gives you the right one — but *only if you knew to include*
$Z$, and *only if* $Z$ is measured.

The other canonical failure is the **collider**:

$$
X \rightarrow Z, \qquad Y \rightarrow Z
$$

If $Z$ is a common effect of $X$ and $Y$, then conditioning on $Z$
*introduces* a spurious association between $X$ and $Y$ where none
existed. This is why "controlling for everything you can measure" is
not a safe strategy — some controls are actively harmful. You need the
graph to tell them apart.

## Why I keep coming back to it

Every serious question about *what to do* is a causal question, not a
predictive one. In trading: what happens to my P&L if I *change* my
execution style? In healthcare: what happens to a patient's outcome if
I *change* their treatment? Prediction alone answers neither. The
value of the causal framework is that it forces you to be explicit
about the structural assumptions that make your answer correct — and
therefore about the assumptions you'd need to challenge to change it.
