Time-to-event data breaks ordinary regression in two places. The response is a
duration, so it is positive and skewed, and the natural object to model is not
its mean but its instantaneous risk of ending,

$$
\lambda(t \mid x) \;=\; \lim_{h\downarrow 0}\frac{1}{h}\,
\mathbb{P}\!\left(t \le T < t+h \;\middle|\; T \ge t,\; x\right).
$$

Worse, some of the durations are not observed at all. A subject who leaves the
study still alive, or a loan still performing when the sample ends, tells you
only that $T$ exceeds some censoring time. Discard those observations and the
sample is biased towards short survivals; keep them and there is nothing to
regress on. Cox's 1972 paper resolves both difficulties with a single
manoeuvre.

## Refusing to name the baseline

The model is

$$
\lambda(t \mid x) \;=\; \lambda_0(t)\,e^{x^{\top}\beta},
$$

a factorisation that puts everything depending on time into $\lambda_0$ and
everything depending on covariates into the exponential. The hazard ratio
between two subjects is $e^{(x_i - x_j)^{\top}\beta}$, constant in $t$: that
proportionality is the model's only structural claim, and everything else is
left free.

Free in a strong sense. $\lambda_0$ is not a parameterised curve with a few
unknowns; it is an arbitrary non-negative function, an infinite-dimensional
nuisance parameter sitting inside the likelihood one would like to maximise.
Maximising over it directly does not work — an unrestricted $\lambda_0$
collapses onto atoms at the observed failure times and drags $\beta$ with it.

## The cancellation

Order the distinct failure times $t_1 < \dots < t_k$ and let $R(t_i)$ be the
risk set, the subjects still under observation and still event-free just before
$t_i$. Rather than asking how likely the observed durations were, ask a much
smaller question: given that exactly one failure occurred at $t_i$, and given
who was available to fail, which of them was it? Under the model that
probability is

$$
\frac{\lambda_0(t_i)\,e^{x_i^{\top}\beta}}
     {\sum_{j \in R(t_i)} \lambda_0(t_i)\,e^{x_j^{\top}\beta}}
\;=\;
\frac{e^{x_i^{\top}\beta}}{\sum_{j \in R(t_i)} e^{x_j^{\top}\beta}},
$$

and the baseline hazard, common to numerator and denominator, is gone. Taking
the product over failure times gives the partial likelihood

$$
L(\beta) \;=\; \prod_{i=1}^{k}
\frac{e^{x_i^{\top}\beta}}{\sum_{j \in R(t_i)} e^{x_j^{\top}\beta}},
$$

in which $\lambda_0$ does not appear anywhere. That is the whole of the trick,
and it pays for two more things without being asked.

Censored subjects are handled by arithmetic rather than by assumption. A
subject censored at $c$ belongs to every risk set before $c$ and none after; it
contributes no factor of its own but constrains everyone else's denominators
for as long as it was genuinely at risk. Nothing about the censoring mechanism
needs modelling beyond its being uninformative about the failure.

And only the ordering of the event times enters. Apply any increasing
transformation to the time axis and $L$ is unchanged, because risk sets are
unchanged. The estimator is a rank statistic wearing a regression's clothes,
which is why it survives a badly chosen time scale.

## A likelihood that is not one

Differentiating gives a score of striking form,

$$
U(\beta) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^{k}\bigl(x_i - \bar{x}(\beta, t_i)\bigr),
\qquad
\bar{x}(\beta,t) \;=\;
\frac{\sum_{j \in R(t)} x_j e^{x_j^{\top}\beta}}
     {\sum_{j \in R(t)} e^{x_j^{\top}\beta}},
$$

observed covariate minus its risk-set expectation, accumulated over events.

But $L$ is not the probability of anything. Its factors condition on
overlapping events, and the terms carrying information about the spacing of
failures have been deliberately thrown away. Cox called it a conditional
likelihood in 1972; the discussion following the paper at the Royal Statistical
Society disputed the name, and he returned to it in 1975 to define partial
likelihood properly. The rigorous asymptotics came later still, from Tsiatis
and from Andersen and Gill, who wrote each subject's event history as a
counting process $N_i$ with intensity $Y_i(t)\lambda_0(t)e^{x_i^{\top}\beta}$
and observed that $U(\beta)$ is a stochastic integral against the compensated
martingales $M_i = N_i - \int Y_i \lambda$. At the true $\beta$ the score is
therefore a martingale with mean zero, and the martingale central limit theorem
delivers normality with the observed information as variance. The usual
likelihood apparatus applies — for a reason that has nothing to do with
likelihood.

The one place the construction shows strain is ties. The derivation assumes
continuous time and so at most one failure per instant, while data arrive
rounded to the day or the month. The exact treatment sums over all orderings
consistent with the observed ties and is combinatorially expensive; Breslow's
approximation reuses one denominator for the whole tied block, and Efron's
discounts it as the block is consumed. Efron's is nearly always the right
default and almost never the reported one.

## The same man's other process

A hazard is the intensity of a point process, and a proportional-hazards
regression is a statement that the intensity factorises into a common shape and
an exponential loading on state. Reduced-form credit modelling makes exactly
that statement, with the default time as the first jump of a doubly stochastic
Poisson process whose intensity is driven by observable factors — a
construction named, for the same author's earlier work on the object, a Cox
process. The two literatures differ mainly in ambition. Survival analysis
estimates the loading and refuses to name the baseline; credit pricing
specifies the baseline as a diffusion and calibrates it to spreads, because it
needs to price rather than to test. It is worth knowing that the honest version
of the problem, the one that admits it cannot see the baseline, still yields a
consistent estimate of everything that varies across names.
