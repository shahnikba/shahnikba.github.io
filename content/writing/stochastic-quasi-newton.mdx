Quasi-Newton methods (BFGS, L-BFGS) work brilliantly when gradients are
exact. Stochastic gradient descent works brilliantly at scale. Combining
them — using curvature information *and* mini-batches — turns out to be
one of the harder problems in optimisation.

The prize: preconditioned steps that converge in fewer iterations than
SGD while remaining scalable. The reality: getting this to work
reliably has taken decades.

## Why the naïve combination fails

Take BFGS and plug in a stochastic gradient. Two things go wrong.

**The secant condition breaks.** BFGS updates $B_{t+1}^{-1}$ using
$s_t = \theta_{t+1} - \theta_t$ and $y_t = \nabla f_{B_t}(\theta_{t+1}) - \nabla f_{B_t}(\theta_t)$.
When the gradients come from *different* mini-batches at the two
points, $y_t$ mixes actual curvature information with sampling noise.
The Hessian approximation drifts unpredictably.

**Positive-definiteness is lost.** BFGS preserves the positive-definite
property of $B_t^{-1}$ only when $s_t^{T} y_t > 0$ — the curvature
condition. Noisy gradients can violate this, at which point the
approximation stops being a valid preconditioner and can send you in
directions that make the loss worse.

## Two families of fixes

**Same-batch differences.** Compute both endpoint gradients on the
*same* mini-batch, so $y_t$ reflects real curvature rather than
sampling variation. Every $k$ steps do a "curvature step": form
$(s_t, y_t)$ on a shared batch, update the approximation. This
family includes SGD-QN and its descendants. Works, but the extra
gradient evaluation and the coordination requirement chip away at the
per-step efficiency SGD gives you for free.

**Fisher-information approximations.** Instead of approximating the
Hessian, approximate the Fisher information matrix — which for many
losses is a good proxy for curvature and has structural properties
(block-diagonal-ish across layers of a neural network) that make it
cheap. **K-FAC** (Kronecker-factored approximate curvature) is the
canonical example: it approximates the Fisher's block structure with
Kronecker products and inverts each block cheaply. Used in some
production settings, especially for reinforcement-learning training
where sample efficiency matters.

## Natural gradient — the parent idea

**Natural gradient** (Amari) is the older, cleaner formulation. If
you view the parameter space as a Riemannian manifold with the
Fisher information as its metric, the "steepest descent" direction is
not $\nabla f$ but $F^{-1} \nabla f$ where $F$ is the Fisher matrix.
This is *reparameterisation-invariant* — the update doesn't depend on
how you happened to parameterise the model — a property that has
real practical value when models are heavily overparameterised.

Every practical stochastic quasi-Newton method is, in some sense, an
approximation to natural gradient. The engineering trick is how to
make $F^{-1}$ tractable.

## Recent momentum in the area

Two lines of work worth noting.

**Sophia** (2023) uses a diagonal Hessian estimate via Hutchinson's
estimator and shows serious speedups on GPT-scale language model
training — a rare regime where anyone has bothered to try
second-order methods at scale. The catch: it requires an extra
backward pass every few steps.

**Shampoo** (2018 onward) is another Fisher-approximation family,
this time using left- and right-side preconditioners. Grinds
through recent large-model benchmarks alongside Sophia and K-FAC,
often with wall-clock wins over Adam in the compute regimes where
extra per-step work is affordable.

## Why they're still not the default

Adam variants dominate deep-learning practice for a boring reason:
they're forgiving. You can grab defaults, plug them in, and get a
result within a factor of two of the best-tuned version. Every
second-order method — natural gradient, K-FAC, L-BFGS variants — is
more sensitive to hyperparameters (particularly the damping term)
and has more room to fail silently. In a world where compute is
cheap and human tuning is expensive, that trade favours the simpler
methods.

The re-emergence of second-order methods at frontier scale is the
first signal that the trade might be shifting: when a single training
run costs millions of dollars, a 30% speed-up justifies a lot of
hyperparameter tuning. Expect this to keep evolving.

## The practical takeaway

- **Medium-scale, batch-mode problems** (calibration, maximum
  likelihood): use L-BFGS. It's usually the right answer and it's in
  every standard library.
- **Large-scale stochastic training** (deep learning): default to
  Adam or well-tuned SGD-with-momentum unless you have a specific
  reason to try K-FAC, Sophia, or Shampoo — and if you do, budget
  time for tuning the damping.
- **Reinforcement learning and policy-gradient methods**: natural
  gradient / K-FAC-style methods are genuinely worth trying because
  the sample-efficiency wins are large.
