Generative models come in a few families with different trade-offs.
GANs generate samples fast but have no tractable likelihood.
Variational autoencoders have a lower-bound on likelihood but
approximate posteriors. **Normalising flows** give you both: exact
likelihood and cheap sampling, from an invertible transformation of a
simple base distribution.

## The change-of-variables formula

Start with a base distribution $p_Z(z)$ (usually a standard Gaussian)
and an invertible, differentiable transformation $x = f_\theta(z)$.
The induced distribution on $x$ has density

$$
p_X(x) \;=\; p_Z(f^{-1}(x)) \, \left|\det \frac{\partial f^{-1}}{\partial x}\right|.
$$

Equivalently, if $z = f^{-1}(x)$:

$$
\log p_X(x) \;=\; \log p_Z(z) + \log \left|\det \frac{\partial f^{-1}(x)}{\partial x}\right|.
$$

You can evaluate the likelihood of any $x$ exactly, and you can
generate samples by drawing $z \sim p_Z$ and applying $f_\theta$.

## The problem to solve

For a single transformation, the Jacobian determinant might be
tractable but the family is limited. To get expressive distributions
you compose many transformations: $x = f_L \circ f_{L-1} \circ \cdots
\circ f_1(z)$. The log-Jacobian sums cleanly across the composition,
but each layer's Jacobian determinant has to be cheap to compute —
otherwise the whole thing costs $O(d^3)$ per layer.

The engineering challenge of normalising flows is designing invertible
layers with cheap Jacobian determinants.

## Coupling layers — RealNVP

**Coupling layers** (Dinh et al., RealNVP) split the input into two
halves $x = (x_a, x_b)$ and transform:

$$
y_a = x_a, \qquad y_b = x_b \odot \exp(s(x_a)) + t(x_a)
$$

where $s$ and $t$ are arbitrary neural networks. The Jacobian is
triangular — half identity, half element-wise scale — so its
determinant is just $\prod \exp(s_i(x_a)) = \exp(\sum s_i(x_a))$.
Cheap.

Alternating which half is transformed layer-to-layer gives you a
composition that can express arbitrarily complex distributions.
RealNVP was the first practical demonstration on high-dimensional
images.

## Autoregressive flows — MAF, IAF

**Autoregressive flows** transform one dimension at a time,
conditioned on previous dimensions:

$$
y_i = \mu_i(x_{<i}) + \sigma_i(x_{<i}) \cdot x_i.
$$

The Jacobian is triangular, determinant is $\prod \sigma_i$. Two
directions:

- **MAF** (Masked Autoregressive Flow): fast density evaluation
  (parallel over dimensions), slow sampling (sequential).
- **IAF** (Inverse Autoregressive Flow): fast sampling, slow density
  evaluation.

Which one you want depends on your use case. For likelihood-based
training on lots of data, MAF wins (evaluation is parallel). For
generation, IAF wins (sampling is parallel).

Both are more expressive per parameter than coupling layers, at the
cost of a more complex implementation.

## Continuous flows — Neural ODE flows

**Continuous normalising flows** replace the discrete composition of
layers with a continuous transformation governed by an ODE:

$$
\frac{dx(t)}{dt} = g_\theta(x(t), t), \qquad x(0) \sim p_Z, \qquad x(1) = \text{sample}.
$$

The change-of-log-density along the trajectory is given by

$$
\frac{d \log p(x(t))}{dt} = -\operatorname{tr}\left(\frac{\partial g}{\partial x}\right)
$$

which is much cheaper than a determinant. In principle this gives
enormous expressivity — you're picking an entire flow field. In
practice the ODE solver at training time is the bottleneck; FFJORD
and successors made this competitive on real problems.

## When normalising flows are the right tool

- **Density estimation.** You want an actual likelihood, not a bound.
  Flows give it to you exactly. Best-in-class on tabular and image
  density estimation among tractable models.
- **Sampling from a hard target with a known density (up to constant).**
  Reverse the perspective: fit a flow to match a target you can
  evaluate, then sample from the flow. Used in physics, Bayesian
  posterior sampling, and variational inference (see: normalising-flow
  VI, IAF-VAEs).
- **Simulating from a learnt model in latent spaces.** Flows compose
  naturally with other generative models — VAEs with flow-based
  priors, for instance.

## The trade-off with GANs

For pure image generation, GANs and their descendants (StyleGAN,
diffusion models) generally beat flows on perceptual quality per
parameter. Flows pay for tractable likelihood by using invertible
architectures, which are less expressive per parameter than
unrestricted neural networks. If you don't need the likelihood, you
don't need to pay that cost.

Where flows win is where the likelihood matters — model selection,
outlier detection, variational inference, physics-informed generative
modelling. That's a real and growing set of applications, even if
image generation gets more attention.
