Reinforcement learning is concerned with an agent attempting to acquire
optimal behaviour in unknown environments that typically exhibit
stochasticity. Though minimally supervised, reinforcement-learning
algorithms have shown numerous successes, ranging from solving Atari
games with Deep Q-Networks, through the triumphant victory against the
world champions at Go, to more recent success in StarCraft.

Maybe the most intuitive formulation of reinforcement learning is the
one stemming from **Andrew and Rich's book** — the introduction of a
reinforcement-learning agent as an actor in a Markov Decision Process,
with the aim of learning an optimal action-selection rule or policy.
Though interesting, with time I came to appreciate **another view** on
reinforcement learning that has roots in probabilistic modelling and
variational inference. I found that such a definition unifies different
views on reinforcement learning under one common framework and gives a
more formal treatment of the field altogether.

To fully understand this formulation, the reader needs to have access
to background on probabilistic modelling, latent-variable models, and
the evidence lower bound (ELBO). As such, I've split the treatment
into two parts: **Part I** covers the background paving the way, and
**Part II** develops the reinforcement-learning formulation itself.

## Part I — the background you'll need

### Latent-variable models

A latent-variable model has observed data $x$ and unobserved
(*latent*) variables $z$, related by a joint

$$
p(x, z) \;=\; p(x \mid z)\, p(z).
$$

The marginal likelihood — the probability of the observations under
the model, integrating out the latents — is

$$
p(x) \;=\; \int p(x \mid z)\, p(z)\, dz.
$$

For all but the simplest models this integral is intractable, and
with it the posterior $p(z \mid x) = p(x, z) / p(x)$.

### The evidence lower bound

Variational inference replaces the intractable posterior with an
optimisation problem: pick a tractable family $q(z)$ and find the
member closest to $p(z \mid x)$ in KL divergence. Algebra rearranges
this into an equivalent maximisation over a quantity we *can*
compute, the **evidence lower bound** (ELBO):

$$
\log p(x) \;\geq\; \mathbb{E}_{q(z)}\!\left[\log p(x, z)\right] - \mathbb{E}_{q(z)}\!\left[\log q(z)\right] \;=\; \mathrm{ELBO}(q).
$$

Maximising the ELBO is equivalent to minimising
$\mathrm{KL}(q \,\|\, p(\cdot \mid x))$. This is the workhorse of
approximate Bayesian inference on models with latent structure.

### Why this matters for RL

The whole apparatus above assumes the setting is *inference over
latents* given observations. Part II shows that reinforcement learning
has exactly the same shape:

- The "observation" becomes an **optimality indicator** we condition on.
- The "latents" become the **trajectories** the agent might traverse.
- The "posterior" becomes the **distribution over optimal
  trajectories**.
- ELBO-style bounds become the objective functions the RL algorithm
  optimises.

Once the mapping is set up, familiar RL results — soft Q-learning,
maximum-entropy policies, Boltzmann exploration — appear as
consequences of the probabilistic formulation rather than as
engineering choices.

## Part II — the reinforcement-learning formulation

### Setup

Fix a graphical model over trajectories $\tau = (s_0, a_0, s_1, a_1, \ldots, s_T, a_T)$
with an extra binary "optimality" variable $O_t \in \{0, 1\}$ at each
timestep. Model the optimality likelihood as an exponential of the
reward:

$$
p(O_t = 1 \mid s_t, a_t) \;=\; \exp(r(s_t, a_t)).
$$

(Rewards are assumed non-positive so this is a valid probability;
subtract the maximum if needed.)

The question we ask is the natural one under this graphical model:
**conditional on all $O_t = 1$, what is the posterior distribution
over trajectories?**

### The posterior falls out as a soft Bellman equation

The posterior over actions at state $s_t$ has the form

$$
p(a_t \mid s_t, O_{t:T} = 1) \;\propto\; \exp\big(Q_{\text{soft}}(s_t, a_t)\big),
$$

where $Q_{\text{soft}}$ satisfies a Bellman-like recursion:

$$
Q_{\text{soft}}(s_t, a_t) \;=\; r(s_t, a_t) + \gamma \, \mathbb{E}_{s_{t+1}}\!\left[V_{\text{soft}}(s_{t+1})\right],
$$

$$
V_{\text{soft}}(s) \;=\; \log \sum_a \exp\big(Q_{\text{soft}}(s, a)\big).
$$

The `log-sum-exp` is a "soft max" — the smooth analogue of the hard
$\max$ in standard Q-learning. It arises here **not as an engineering
choice but as the marginal likelihood of the graphical model**.

### What falls out for free

**Maximum-entropy RL.** The optimal posterior policy under this
model maximises expected reward *plus* an entropy bonus
$-\log \pi(a \mid s)$. In the classical control framing, entropy
regularisation is a heuristic often added for exploration; in the
inference framing, it is *the* correct objective for computing the
posterior over actions. Soft Actor–Critic and related methods aren't
ad hoc — they're principled approximations to this posterior.

**Boltzmann exploration is the right policy, not a heuristic.**
Softmax-over-Q with temperature 1 is the exact posterior policy in
this graphical model. Temperature scaling corresponds to rescaling
the reward magnitude. That gives a principled temperature schedule:
anneal by scaling the reward.

**Off-policy evaluation as Bayesian inference.** The whole apparatus
of importance-weighted inference — sequential Monte Carlo, particle
filters, variational bounds — carries over to policy evaluation.
This is where the framing pays real dividends for high-variance,
long-horizon estimators.

### The relationship to imitation learning and inverse RL

The inference framing makes imitation learning fit alongside RL
cleanly. Instead of conditioning on optimality $O_t = 1$, condition
on observed expert actions $a_t = a_t^{\text{expert}}$. The
posterior over the agent's policy given the expert trajectory is a
well-posed inference problem, and gradient descent on the
log-posterior recovers behavioural cloning as a special case, with
principled uncertainty quantification and regularisation coming for
free.

Inverse reinforcement learning — inferring the reward from expert
trajectories — is the same graphical model with the reward function
itself as the latent variable to be inferred.

## The broader lesson

Recasting a familiar problem as inference under a different generative
model often reveals that ad hoc engineering choices are consequences
of the reformulation — and suggests new choices you hadn't seen before.
The RL-as-inference story is one of the cleaner examples in modern
machine learning of that pattern.
