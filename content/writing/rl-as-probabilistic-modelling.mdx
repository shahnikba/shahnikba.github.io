The classical framing of reinforcement learning is control: an agent
picks actions to maximise expected discounted reward in an MDP.
There's a second framing that keeps growing in influence — recasting
the same problem as **probabilistic inference over trajectories**.

The two framings are equivalent in a specific sense. But viewed
through the probabilistic lens, several ideas that seem ad hoc in the
control framing turn out to be natural consequences of Bayesian
reasoning.

## The setup

Fix a graphical model over trajectories with three kinds of variables:
states $s_t$, actions $a_t$, and an "optimality" indicator $O_t \in
\{0, 1\}$ at each timestep. Model the optimality likelihood as an
exponential of the reward:

$$
p(O_t = 1 \mid s_t, a_t) \;=\; \exp(r(s_t, a_t)).
$$

(Reward is assumed non-positive so this is a valid probability; you
can always shift.)

Now ask the natural question: **conditional on all $O_t = 1$, what is
the posterior distribution over trajectories?**

## What falls out

The posterior over actions has a clean form:

$$
p(a_t \mid s_t, O_{t:T} = 1) \;\propto\; \exp\big(Q_{\text{soft}}(s_t, a_t)\big)
$$

where $Q_{\text{soft}}$ is the **soft Q-function** satisfying a
Bellman-like recursion:

$$
Q_{\text{soft}}(s_t, a_t) \;=\; r(s_t, a_t) + \gamma \, \mathbb{E}_{s_{t+1}}\!\left[V_{\text{soft}}(s_{t+1})\right],
$$

$$
V_{\text{soft}}(s) \;=\; \log \sum_a \exp\big(Q_{\text{soft}}(s, a)\big).
$$

The `log-sum-exp` is a "soft max" — literally the smooth analogue of
the hard $\max$ in standard Q-learning. It arises here **not as an
engineering choice but as the marginal likelihood of the graphical
model**.

## What this reframing gives you

Three practical consequences.

**Maximum-entropy RL comes for free.** The optimal posterior policy
maximises expected reward *plus* an entropy bonus $-\log \pi(a
\mid s)$. In the control framing, entropy regularisation is a
heuristic often added for exploration; in the inference framing, it
is *the* correct objective for computing the posterior over actions.
Soft Actor-Critic and related methods aren't ad hoc — they're the
principled approximation.

**Boltzmann exploration is the right policy, not just a heuristic.**
Softmax-over-Q with temperature 1 is the exact posterior policy in
this graphical model. Temperature scaling corresponds to raising or
lowering the reward magnitude. This gives a principled temperature
schedule: anneal by scaling reward.

**Off-policy evaluation gets a clean Bayesian formulation.** The
whole apparatus of importance-weighted inference — SMC, particle
filters, variational bounds — carries over to policy evaluation. This
is where the framing pays real dividends for high-variance long-
horizon estimators.

## Where the framings differ

They're equivalent in the limit but they differ in what they make
easy to reason about.

**Control framing is natural when:**
- You care about a hard-optimal deterministic policy.
- The reward structure is well-defined and you want an
  interpretable value function.
- You need standard convergence guarantees (Q-learning, policy
  gradient theorems).

**Inference framing is natural when:**
- You want stochastic policies as a first-class object (for
  exploration, for robustness, for imitation learning where the demo
  policy is stochastic).
- You want to combine reward-optimising and imitation objectives
  cleanly — as in inverse RL, where the reward function itself is
  a hidden variable to infer.
- You want the entropy-regularised objective without justifying it as
  a hack.

## The relationship to imitation learning

The inference framing makes imitation learning fit alongside RL
cleanly. Instead of optimality $O_t = 1$, condition on observed
expert actions $a_t = a_t^{\text{expert}}$. The posterior over the
agent's policy given the expert trajectory is a well-posed inference
problem, and gradient descent on the log-posterior recovers standard
behavioural cloning as a special case, with principled
uncertainty-quantification and regularisation coming for free.

Inverse reinforcement learning — inferring the reward from expert
trajectories — is the same graphical model with the reward function
itself as the latent variable. What was two separate research
communities in the 2010s became one under the probabilistic-inference
banner.

## The broader lesson

Recasting a familiar problem as inference under a different
generative model often reveals that ad hoc engineering choices are
consequences of the reformulation — and suggests new choices you
hadn't seen before. The RL-as-inference story is one of the cleaner
examples in modern machine learning of that pattern.
