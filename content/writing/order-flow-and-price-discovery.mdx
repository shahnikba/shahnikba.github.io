The classical account has prices move because value moves, and trading as the
mechanism by which the move is registered. Microstructure inverts the order of
explanation. There is no observable value; there is a sequence of orders, and
the price is whatever the mechanism produces from them. For anyone quoting a
currency pair electronically this is not a philosophical preference. The
dealer's information set is exactly the message stream — limit orders, market
orders, cancellations, sizes, timestamps — and every quoting decision is an
inference from it.

Hasbrouck's *Empirical Market Microstructure* is the book that makes that
inference statistical. It contains almost no stochastic calculus and very few
options. What it has instead is a series of models simple enough to be fitted
to transaction data, each one designed to recover a quantity a dealer actually
needs: how much of the spread is compensation for being picked off, how much of
a price move will persist, how much information a given trade carried.

## The signed trade as the primitive

Everything begins with signing the tape. Write

$$
q_t=\begin{cases}+1 & \text{buyer-initiated}\\[2pt] -1 & \text{seller-initiated}\end{cases}
\qquad\text{or, size-weighted,}\qquad x_t = q_t V_t .
$$

Roll's model is the cleanest demonstration that this variable alone carries
structure. Let the efficient price be a random walk, $m_t = m_{t-1} +
\varepsilon_t$, and let transactions occur at a fixed half-spread either side,

$$
p_t = m_t + c\,q_t ,
$$

with $q_t$ serially independent and independent of $\varepsilon$. Then

$$
\Delta p_t = c\,(q_t-q_{t-1}) + \varepsilon_t ,
$$

and the first autocovariance of returns is

$$
\gamma_1 = \operatorname{Cov}(\Delta p_t,\Delta p_{t-1}) = -c^2 ,
$$

so the effective spread is $2c = 2\sqrt{-\gamma_1}$. The bid–offer is
recoverable from a price series in which no quote was ever recorded. Bounce
between the two sides of the book induces negative autocorrelation, and the
magnitude of that autocorrelation is the cost of crossing. It is a small
result, and it sets the pattern for the rest of the book: choose a structural
story, derive its signature in a second moment, invert.

## Decomposing the spread

The spread is not zero, and Roll's $c$ does not say why. Three costs sit inside
it. Order processing — servers, connectivity, the exchange's fees — is real but
static and, by the early 2010s, small. Inventory cost is the price of holding
what the flow has left you with: after a run of client buying you are long EUR
against USD, and rather than quoting symmetrically you shade both sides down,
discouraging further buyers and paying up for sellers. That is the reservation
price of the [Avellaneda–Stoikov problem](/writing/avellaneda-stoikov/),
arrived at from the empirical side.

The third is the dangerous one. In the Glosten–Milgrom account the dealer quotes
without regret, meaning each side is a conditional expectation given the trade
it would attract:

$$
a_t = \mathbb{E}\!\left[V \mid \mathcal{F}_{t-1}, \text{buy at } t\right],
\qquad
b_t = \mathbb{E}\!\left[V \mid \mathcal{F}_{t-1}, \text{sell at } t\right].
$$

Since a buy is evidence of good news and a sale evidence of bad, $a_t > b_t$
even with no processing cost, no inventory, and a risk-neutral dealer. The
spread exists because trading is informative. A dealer who quotes inside it is
not being competitive; he is subsidising whoever knows more.

Adverse selection also separates cleanly from the transitory costs in the
second moments. Let the efficient price now update on trade innovations,

$$
m_t = m_{t-1} + \lambda\,q_t + \varepsilon_t ,\qquad p_t = m_t + c\,q_t ,
$$

so that

$$
\Delta p_t = (\lambda + c)\,q_t - c\,q_{t-1} + \varepsilon_t .
$$

The half-spread is $\lambda + c$, the return autocovariance is $\gamma_1 =
-c(\lambda+c)$, and the two parameters are identified from the variance and
first autocovariance of returns together. The ratio $\lambda/(\lambda+c)$ is the
fraction of the half-spread that is pure information cost — the part that
cannot be competed away, because widening it is the only defence against being
run over.

## Impact that persists and impact that does not

The same $\lambda$ is Kyle's. In the single-period model with terminal value
$v \sim N(p_0,\Sigma_0)$ and uninformed order flow $u \sim N(0,\sigma_u^2)$, the
market maker's equilibrium pricing rule is linear in total order flow,
$\Delta p = \lambda\,x$, with

$$
\lambda = \frac{1}{2}\,\frac{\sqrt{\Sigma_0}}{\sigma_u} .
$$

Depth $1/\lambda$ falls as the informed trader's advantage grows and rises with
the volume of trade that carries no information at all. Read as an operating
instruction: when estimated $\lambda$ rises, quote smaller and wider, because
the same clip is now moving the market further and the move is more likely to be
against you.

The distinction that matters on a desk is not size of impact but persistence.
Decompose the transaction price into a martingale and a stationary residual,

$$
p_t = m_t + s_t ,\qquad m_t = m_{t-1} + w_t ,
$$

where $m_t$ is the efficient price and $s_t$ the pricing error. Impact routed
through $w_t$ is permanent: the market has revised its view and the level will
not come back. Impact routed through $s_t$ is transitory: liquidity was consumed
and will be replenished, and the price reverts as it is. A dealer filled against
temporary impact has earned the spread on a position that mean-reverts into
profit. A dealer filled against permanent impact has sold something that is
about to be worth more. The variance $\sigma_w^2$ measures how much information
the session produced; the dispersion of $s_t$ measures how far the mechanism
strayed from it.

## Measuring what a trade knew

Serially independent $q_t$ is a fiction — large orders are split, and signed
flow is strongly autocorrelated at every horizon anyone has looked at. Hasbrouck's
answer is a bivariate vector autoregression in quote revisions $r_t$ and signed
trades $x_t$,

$$
r_t = \sum_{i\ge 1} a_i\,r_{t-i} + \sum_{i\ge 0} b_i\,x_{t-i} + v_{1,t},
\qquad
x_t = \sum_{i\ge 1} c_i\,r_{t-i} + \sum_{i\ge 1} d_i\,x_{t-i} + v_{2,t}.
$$

The asymmetry in the summation limits is the identifying assumption: a trade may
move the quote within the same interval, a quote may not move the trade that
preceded it. What is then interesting is not any single coefficient but the
cumulative impulse response of returns to a trade innovation,

$$
\alpha = \lim_{n\to\infty}\ \mathbb{E}\!\left[\sum_{i=0}^{n} r_{t+i}\ \middle|\ v_{2,t}\right],
$$

which is the permanent price revision attributable to an unexpected trade, net of
everything the flow itself had already made predictable. This is the quantity
worth calling the information content of a trade, and it is why a hundred
thousand of retail flow and two hundred million from a fund that trades once a
month are not comparable objects. The same machinery splits $\sigma_w^2$ into a
trade-correlated part and a residual: the first is price discovery happening
through the book, the second is public news arriving over the top of it.

## What this was for, in 2013

On EBS, Reuters Matching and Hotspot the quoting problem had stopped being the
construction of a rate from an interbank mid and become the continuous
interpretation of a message stream. The loop that resulted was an estimation
problem wrapped around a control problem: infer fair value from recent quotes,
measure signed flow, mark inventory, estimate the current adverse-selection
intensity and the current $\lambda$, and only then solve for bid and ask. The
first five steps are Hasbrouck. The last is Avellaneda–Stoikov. Neither is much
use without the other — one says what should be measured, the other what to do
once it has been.

The reason this mattered in currencies specifically was Evans and Lyons, who
found that daily exchange rate variation was explained substantially better by
cumulated interdealer order flow than by contemporaneous macroeconomic news.
For a market whose textbook account was entirely about interest differentials
and announcements, that was a considerable result, and it made the microstructure
programme the natural framework for FX rather than an equities import.

Everything since has been refinement of the same primitive. Order-book
imbalance, signed volume, queue imbalance, trade intensity, VPIN, cumulative
flow: these are functions of $q_t$ and its history, and whether they are consumed
by a linear model or a network changes the estimator without changing what is
being estimated. The features have outlived the models that first justified
them, which is the usual sign that the framing was right.
