Take the Heston stochastic-local volatility model,

$$
dS_t = \mu S_t\,dt + L(S_t,t)\sqrt{V_t}\,S_t\,dW^1_t,
\qquad
dV_t = \kappa(\theta - V_t)\,dt + \xi\sqrt{V_t}\,dW^2_t,
$$

with $dW^1_t\,dW^2_t = \rho\,dt$ and $(\kappa,\theta,\xi,\rho)$ already
calibrated to the term structure of the smile dynamics. The only unknown
left is the leverage function $L(S,t)$, and the whole of what the industry
calls "SLV calibration" is the business of finding it.

## The problem is one conditional expectation

[Gyöngy's mimicking theorem](/writing/markovian-projection/) says there is a
local volatility diffusion with the same one-dimensional marginals as the
model above provided

$$
\sigma_{LV}^2(S,t) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[L^2(S_t,t)\,V_t \mid S_t = S\right].
$$

Because $L$ is a deterministic function of $S$ and $t$ it steps outside the
expectation, and the calibration condition collapses to

$$
L^2(S,t) = \frac{\sigma_{LV}^2(S,t)}{m(S,t)},
\qquad
m(S,t) = \mathbb{E}[V_t \mid S_t = S].
$$

That is the entire problem. Dupire hands you $\sigma_{LV}$ from the market;
everything else in the literature is machinery for computing one conditional
expectation.

It is not a closed-form computation, because $m$ is taken under the law of a
process whose diffusion coefficient contains $L$. So $L$ determines $m$
determines $L$: a nonlinear fixed point, solved by iterating from an initial
guess — $L^{(0)} \equiv 1$ works — and marching forward in time, since
$m(\cdot,t)$ only ever depends on the leverage function up to $t$.

## Carrying the law as particles

The first way to compute $m$ is to simulate. Run $N$ paths of
$(S_t,V_t)$ under the current leverage function; at each maturity you hold a
cloud of pairs $\{(S_i,V_i)\}$, which is to say the empirical measure

$$
\mu_N = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\delta_{(S_i,V_i)}.
$$

The conditional expectation is then a regression problem. With a Gaussian
kernel of bandwidth $h$,

$$
\widehat{m}(S) = \frac{\sum_i V_i\,K_h(S_i - S)}{\sum_i K_h(S_i - S)},
$$

and $L$ is updated as $\sigma_{LV}/\sqrt{\widehat{m}}$ before the next sweep.

The appeal is that it needs nothing you do not already own. Any Monte Carlo
engine that prices under the stochastic volatility model will do, and the
recipe generalises to almost any choice of variance process. The cost is
statistical: error decaying as $N^{-1/2}$, a bandwidth that trades bias
against variance, and — worst of all — thin sampling in the wings, which is
exactly where the leverage function is least well determined and most
consequential for barriers.

## Carrying the law as a density

Ren, Madan and Qian make the opposite choice: represent the same law by its
density $p(S,V,t)$, which satisfies the forward Kolmogorov equation
$\partial_t p = \mathcal{L}^\ast p$, written out as

$$
\begin{aligned}
\frac{\partial p}{\partial t} =\;
&-\frac{\partial}{\partial S}(\mu S p)
-\frac{\partial}{\partial V}\big(\kappa(\theta - V)p\big) \\
&+\tfrac12 \frac{\partial^2}{\partial S^2}\big(L^2 V S^2 p\big)
+\frac{\partial^2}{\partial S\,\partial V}\big(\rho\xi L V S p\big)
+\tfrac12 \frac{\partial^2}{\partial V^2}\big(\xi^2 V p\big).
\end{aligned}
$$

The nonlinearity is visible in the coefficients, but it is benign in
practice: over a single time step $L$ is known from the step before, so each
step is a linear parabolic solve and ADI handles it. From the density,

$$
m(S,t) = \frac{\int V p(S,V,t)\,dV}{\int p(S,V,t)\,dV},
$$

which is quadrature, not statistics. No kernel, no bandwidth, no estimator
variance; the error is discretisation error in the mesh. What you pay for it
is a two-dimensional grid at every step, awkward boundary treatment, and a
variance grid that has to cope with the behaviour of the Heston density near
the origin when the Feller condition is violated.

## The same fixed point twice

Both methods are the same map. Given $L$, both build the law
$\mathbb{P}_L(S_t,V_t)$, both extract $\mathbb{E}[V_t\mid S_t = S]$, and both
return $\sigma_{LV}/\sqrt{m}$. The only thing that differs is how the measure
is carried between those steps — as a sum of point masses, or as a density on
a grid. Choosing between them is not choosing an algorithm so much as
choosing which error you would rather be responsible for: sampling error you
can only beat down with paths, or mesh error you can beat down with
refinement.

That framing is worth holding on to, because it tells you what a genuinely
new method would have to do. It would have to represent $\mathbb{P}_L$ some
third way.

---

**Addendum, 2019.** Something did. Guyon and Henry-Labordère's particle
method — circulated as a working paper and then published in *Risk* in
January 2012, with the fuller treatment in *Nonlinear Option Pricing* —
keeps the empirical measure but drops the independence of the paths. The
conditional expectation estimated from the current cloud feeds straight back
into the coefficients governing every path, so the system is a McKean–Vlasov
interacting particle system rather than a set of independent trajectories,
and the leverage function is built on the fly in a single forward pass
instead of by repeated simulation to convergence. In the language above it is
a third representation of $\mathbb{P}_L$, sitting between the empirical
measure and the density: still particles, but self-consistent ones, and
accurate at path counts the naive kernel scheme would find embarrassing. It
is now the default in most production SLV calibrators I have seen. None of
the section above changes; it simply acquires a third column.
