These are my working notes on variational inference, distilled
from Carl Doersch's *Tutorial on Variational Autoencoders* and
Taehwan Kim's VI lecture notes (both linked at the bottom).

Bayesian posterior inference — computing $p(z \mid x)$ — is almost
always intractable for interesting models. Variational inference is
the pragmatic answer: replace the intractable integral with an
**optimisation problem**.

The move is to pick a family $\mathcal{Q}$ of tractable distributions
$q(z)$ and find the member of $\mathcal{Q}$ closest to the true
posterior in KL divergence:

$$
q^{*}(z) = \arg\min_{q \in \mathcal{Q}} \; \mathrm{KL}\!\left[q(z) \,\|\, p(z \mid x)\right].
$$

The KL still involves the intractable posterior, but the elegant part
is that minimising it is equivalent to *maximising* the **evidence
lower bound** (ELBO), which involves only quantities we can compute:

$$
\mathrm{ELBO}(q) = \mathbb{E}_{q(z)}\!\left[\log p(x, z)\right] - \mathbb{E}_{q(z)}\!\left[\log q(z)\right].
$$

Now you have a well-posed optimisation. Everything downstream —
mean-field VI, black-box VI, VAEs — is a specific choice of
$\mathcal{Q}$ and a specific way to compute or estimate the ELBO
gradient.

## The KL asymmetry matters

Variational inference minimises $\mathrm{KL}(q \| p)$ — the "reverse"
KL. This has a particular geometric consequence: the optimal $q$ is
**mode-seeking**. It concentrates on one mode of the true posterior
and ignores others rather than smearing itself across multiple modes.
If the true posterior is multimodal, standard VI misses the
multimodality entirely.

The "forward" KL, $\mathrm{KL}(p \| q)$, is **mode-covering** — it
would penalise $q$ heavily for putting zero probability where $p$ has
support, forcing $q$ to spread across modes. It's what expectation
propagation minimises, and what you'd get from importance sampling
with $p$ as the target.

Neither direction is uniformly better. Mode-seeking gives you cleaner
posteriors on unimodal problems and often better predictive
performance; mode-covering gives you honest coverage on multimodal
problems. Which one you want depends on what you'll do with the
posterior.

## Mean-field VI — the classical special case

The simplest variational family is **mean-field**: assume the
posterior factorises over dimensions:

$$
q(z_1, \ldots, z_D) = \prod_{i=1}^{D} q_i(z_i).
$$

For models with conjugate exponential-family conditionals, coordinate-
ascent VI gives clean closed-form updates for each $q_i$, cycling
through dimensions like Gibbs sampling but for parameters of $q$
rather than samples.

Mean-field is fast and often accurate enough for descriptive purposes.
Its systematic failure mode is *variance underestimation* — by
assuming independence, it can't represent posterior correlations, and
tends to concentrate the marginals too tightly around the mode. If you
compare mean-field VI posteriors to MCMC posteriors, VI's are almost
always narrower.

## Black-box VI — the deep-learning fix

For non-conjugate models (which is almost anything interesting),
closed-form coordinate ascent isn't available. **Black-box VI**
(Ranganath et al.) uses stochastic gradient estimates of the ELBO,
plugging in whatever $q$ family you like and any $p(x, z)$ you can
evaluate.

The ELBO gradient via the log-derivative trick:

$$
\nabla_{\phi} \mathrm{ELBO}(q_\phi) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z)}\!\left[\big(\log p(x, z) - \log q_\phi(z)\big) \nabla_\phi \log q_\phi(z)\right].
$$

Estimate with samples. High variance in general; every subsequent
method (control variates, Rao-Blackwellisation, reparameterisation) is
some strategy for reducing that variance.

## The reparameterisation trick — VAEs

For continuous $z$ and reparameterisable $q$ families, there's a
lower-variance alternative. If we can write $z = g_\phi(\epsilon)$ for
some noise $\epsilon \sim p(\epsilon)$ independent of $\phi$ (e.g.,
$z = \mu_\phi + \sigma_\phi \odot \epsilon$ for Gaussian $q$), then

$$
\nabla_\phi \mathbb{E}_{q_\phi(z)}[f(z)] \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{p(\epsilon)}[\nabla_\phi f(g_\phi(\epsilon))].
$$

The gradient goes *through* the sampling step because the randomness
is external. This gives dramatically lower-variance estimators, which
is what makes variational autoencoders trainable at scale.

The VAE is exactly mean-field VI over an amortised $q$ family:
instead of learning $q_i$ per data point $x_i$, learn a single
"encoder" $q_\phi(z \mid x)$ that outputs the variational parameters
as a neural-network function of $x$. Amortisation trades approximation
error (a fixed-capacity encoder is worse than dedicated variational
parameters per point) for enormous computational savings.

## When VI is the wrong tool

Three settings where I'd reach for MCMC (or a diffusion-based
alternative) instead:

- **Multimodal posteriors** where all modes matter.
- **Small models with well-defined priors** where exact HMC-quality
  inference is affordable and the certainty premium justifies the
  compute.
- **Model comparison via marginal likelihood** — VI gives you a lower
  bound on $\log p(x)$, not the marginal likelihood itself.
  Bridge sampling or nested sampling are more honest.

For deep generative models on high-dimensional data (images, audio,
scientific fields), VI variants remain competitive because the
alternatives don't scale. But it's worth remembering what you gave
up when you chose the ELBO.
