Bayesian posterior inference — computing $p(z \mid x)$ — is almost
always intractable for interesting models. Variational inference is
the pragmatic answer: replace the intractable integral with an
**optimisation problem**.

The move is: pick a family $\mathcal{Q}$ of tractable distributions
$q(z)$ and find the member of $\mathcal{Q}$ closest to the true
posterior in KL divergence:

$$
q^{*}(z) = \arg\min_{q \in \mathcal{Q}} \; \mathrm{KL}\big[q(z) \,\|\, p(z \mid x)\big].
$$

The KL divergence still involves the intractable posterior. The
elegant part: minimising this KL is equivalent to *maximising* the
**evidence lower bound** (ELBO), which only involves quantities we
can compute:

$$
\mathrm{ELBO}(q) = \mathbb{E}_{q(z)}\big[\log p(x, z)\big] - \mathbb{E}_{q(z)}\big[\log q(z)\big].
$$

Now you have a well-posed optimisation. Everything downstream —
mean-field VI, black-box VI, VAEs — is a specific choice of
$\mathcal{Q}$ and a specific way to compute or estimate the ELBO
gradient.
