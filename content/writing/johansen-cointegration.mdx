For much of the late 2000s and early 2010s, one of the workhorse
relative-value strategies on credit prop desks was to build
portfolios of CDS names whose relative values kept coming back to a
stable equilibrium — even when the individual spreads were free to
wander. The technical machinery behind those baskets was almost
always Johansen's multivariate cointegration test.

The setup is direct. Take a set of similar issuers — European
banks, US investment-grade industrials, European autos, a sovereign
cluster. Each individual CDS spread is well-approximated as an
integrated process: it drifts with the credit cycle, with funding
conditions, with sector-wide news. But because these issuers share
those common drivers, certain weighted combinations of their
spreads turn out to be remarkably stable. The whole game is finding
the weights.

## Why cointegration, not correlation

The obvious tempting shortcut is to use correlation. Two CDS
spreads with a 95% correlation must be substitutes for each other,
right? Not quite. Correlation is a statement about short-term
co-movement in changes; it tells you nothing about whether the
levels drift apart over time.

Cointegration is a statement about the long-run equilibrium. Two
spreads can move together day-to-day and yet slowly separate over
months. Only a cointegrated linear combination is guaranteed to be
stationary — and only a stationary spread is a legitimate
mean-reversion target.

## The Johansen framework in one page

Start with the observed vector
$X_t = (CDS_1, CDS_2, \ldots, CDS_n)^{\top}$, each component
approximately $I(1)$. Model it as a vector autoregression and
rewrite in Vector Error Correction form:

$$
\Delta X_t = \Pi X_{t-1} + \sum_{i=1}^{p-1} \Gamma_i \Delta X_{t-i} + \epsilon_t.
$$

Everything interesting is inside $\Pi$. Johansen's decomposition
splits it as $\Pi = \alpha \beta^{\top}$, where the columns of
$\beta$ are the *cointegration vectors* — the basket weights that
make the combination stationary — and $\alpha$ contains the
*adjustment speeds*: which of the underlying names does the
pulling back when the spread deviates.

The number of independent stationary combinations — the rank of
$\Pi$ — is estimated with a trace test or a maximum-eigenvalue
test. Most desks trade the single dominant relationship even when
more exist, because interpretability matters as much as
statistical power.

## Reading β and α practically

A fitted $\beta$ for, say, five European banks might look like

$$
\beta = (1,\ -0.72,\ -0.54,\ 0.18,\ 0.08)^{\top}.
$$

That defines the basket: long one unit of CDS A, short 0.72 of
CDS B, short 0.54 of CDS C, long a small amount of D and E. These
are the hedge ratios — the trade you actually put on.

$\alpha$ is a diagnostic. A large adjustment coefficient for name B
tells you that when the basket dislocates, it's usually B that
snaps back into line. Useful for sizing, for choosing which leg to
hedge more aggressively, and — most importantly — for spotting when
the adjustment mechanism itself is degrading.

## The trading loop

The rest is discipline. Once you have $\beta$ and have confirmed
the constructed spread $S_t = \beta^{\top} X_t$ is stationary
(ADF or KPSS on the fitted series), estimate its mean, standard
deviation and half-life. Standardise the current level as a
z-score,

$$
z_t = \frac{S_t - \mu}{\sigma},
$$

enter when $|z_t|$ crosses a threshold — two standard deviations
is the classical starting point, adapted to the half-life and the
transaction costs — and exit as the z-score reverts towards zero.
No directional view on credit is taken; the trade depends only on
the relationship reasserting itself.

## Where it breaks

The reason this strategy needs constant re-estimation is that the
equilibrium itself changes. Mergers, downgrades, spinoffs,
restructurings all break the industrial logic that made a basket
cohere in the first place. Regime shifts — the 2008 crisis and the
2010–12 European sovereign debt crisis both did this — re-price
credit correlations wholesale and turn a stable cointegration
vector into a slowly-drifting one that hasn't quite blown up yet
but has stopped meaning what it used to. And when one leg's
liquidity deteriorates, execution slippage quietly overwhelms the
small z-score reversion gains that make the whole thing profitable.

The cointegration relationship is a living thing. Baskets get
re-fitted regularly, tested rigorously out of sample, and pulled
the moment the underlying economic story stops holding up. It's
simple statistical machinery, hard market realities, and the
reason cointegration-based baskets stayed a staple of credit
relative-value trading for the better part of two decades — until
Volcker and post-crisis regulation squeezed the space they lived
in.
