A **hidden Markov model** has two ingredients: a Markov chain over
hidden states you cannot observe, and an emission distribution that
generates observations conditional on the current hidden state. You
see the emissions; you infer the states.

Concretely, an HMM is defined by:

- A set of hidden states $\{1, \ldots, K\}$
- A **transition matrix** $A_{ij} = P(z_{t+1} = j \mid z_t = i)$
- An **initial distribution** $\pi_i = P(z_1 = i)$
- **Emission distributions** $B_i(x) = P(x_t \mid z_t = i)$ —
  Gaussian, discrete, or whatever your observations look like

That's it. The joint density factorises as

$$
P(x_{1:T}, z_{1:T}) = \pi_{z_1} \prod_{t=1}^{T} B_{z_t}(x_t) \prod_{t=1}^{T-1} A_{z_t, z_{t+1}}.
$$

## The three canonical questions

**1. Evaluation.** Given a model $\lambda = (A, B, \pi)$ and observation
sequence $x_{1:T}$, what is $P(x_{1:T} \mid \lambda)$?

Naïve marginalisation would sum over $K^T$ hidden-state sequences.
The **forward algorithm** does it in $O(K^2 T)$ via dynamic programming:

$$
\alpha_t(i) = P(x_{1:t}, z_t = i \mid \lambda),
$$

$$
\alpha_1(i) = \pi_i B_i(x_1),
$$

$$
\alpha_{t+1}(j) = B_j(x_{t+1}) \sum_i \alpha_t(i) A_{ij}.
$$

The marginal likelihood is $P(x_{1:T} \mid \lambda) = \sum_i \alpha_T(i)$.

**2. Decoding.** What's the most likely sequence of hidden states?
This is the **Viterbi algorithm** — same shape as forward, but with
$\max$ instead of $\sum$:

$$
\delta_t(i) = \max_{z_{1:t-1}} P(x_{1:t}, z_{1:t-1}, z_t = i \mid \lambda),
$$

$$
\delta_{t+1}(j) = B_j(x_{t+1}) \max_i \big[\delta_t(i) A_{ij}\big].
$$

Track back-pointers to recover the argmax sequence. Also $O(K^2 T)$.

**3. Learning.** Given only observations, estimate $\lambda = (A, B, \pi)$.
This is **Baum–Welch**, an instance of EM.

- **E-step**: use forward-backward to compute the posterior over
  hidden states,
  $$\gamma_t(i) = P(z_t = i \mid x_{1:T}, \lambda),$$
  and the two-slice posteriors $\xi_t(i, j) = P(z_t = i, z_{t+1} = j
  \mid x_{1:T}, \lambda)$.
- **M-step**: re-estimate the parameters by weighted MLE using
  $\gamma$ and $\xi$ as the sufficient statistics.

Converges to a local optimum of the observed-data likelihood; sensitive
to initialisation.

## Backward and forward-backward

The **backward algorithm** is the sibling of forward:

$$
\beta_t(i) = P(x_{t+1:T} \mid z_t = i, \lambda),
$$

$$
\beta_T(i) = 1,
$$

$$
\beta_t(i) = \sum_j A_{ij} B_j(x_{t+1}) \beta_{t+1}(j).
$$

The state posterior at any timestep combines both:

$$
\gamma_t(i) = \frac{\alpha_t(i) \beta_t(i)}{P(x_{1:T} \mid \lambda)}.
$$

This is the "forward-backward" step used inside Baum–Welch's E-step
and inside standard smoothing for online HMMs.

## When to use HMMs today

HMMs were the workhorse of speech recognition for decades, until
sequence-to-sequence neural networks displaced them around 2015. In
pure prediction accuracy on rich data, deep sequence models win.
HMMs still earn their keep in specific settings:

- **Small labelled datasets** where a low-parameter model with strong
  inductive bias generalises better than a deep network. Bioinformatics
  is full of these.
- **Interpretability of latent structure.** HMMs give you an explicit
  posterior over discrete states — very useful if the "state" is
  meaningful (regime detection in finance, patient-state modelling in
  clinical trajectories).
- **Streaming / online settings** where the forward algorithm's
  per-timestep constant cost is exactly what you want.

## The finance connection — regime-switching models

Regime-switching models (Hamilton, 1989) are HMMs with continuous
emissions used to identify latent market regimes. The states might
correspond to "high volatility", "trending", "range-bound" — the
model doesn't label them for you; you interpret the fitted states
post hoc.

Practically these models are noisier than they look — the state
posterior is often diffuse, regime boundaries shift as you add data,
and out-of-sample regime prediction is difficult. But as a
descriptive tool for characterising historical regimes, they're
useful, and the framework generalises cleanly to state-space
models with continuous states (Kalman filters), which are more
directly useful.

## What HMMs teach beyond HMMs

The forward-backward algorithm is the discrete-state ancestor of the
Kalman filter (continuous linear-Gaussian states) and of belief
propagation on graphs generally. Once you've internalised the
message-passing pattern in HMMs, both Kalman filtering and modern
graphical-model inference feel like variations on the same theme.
That transferable intuition is why HMMs deserve a first-class place
in the curriculum long after they've stopped being state-of-the-art.
