Reinforcement learning is a machine that answers **rung-2** questions:
if I take action $a$ in state $s$, what reward do I expect? Its native
setting is *interventional* — the agent gets to try things.

Causal inference lives on all three rungs of Pearl's ladder —
association, intervention, counterfactuals — but its methods are built
around **observational** data, where the agent didn't get to choose
what happened.

The tension between the two matters more than it looks.

## Where standard RL breaks

Textbook RL assumes the agent controls the actions. Two situations
break that assumption in practice:

**Off-policy learning from logged data.** You have a dataset of
$(s, a, r, s')$ tuples collected by *some other* policy $\pi_b$ — a
human operator, a previous algorithm, a random exploration policy —
and you want to evaluate or improve a different policy $\pi$. Now
the actions in your data weren't chosen by you, and the confounders
that made $\pi_b$ pick $a$ at state $s$ may also affect the reward.
Importance-sampling corrections handle this *if* $\pi_b$ is known and
overlapping — otherwise you have an unobserved-confounder problem
straight out of causal inference.

**Partial observability of the state.** In practical MDPs the "state"
you condition on is a projection of the true underlying state. If a
latent variable affects both the action selection *and* the reward,
you have unmeasured confounding, and value estimates from
observational trajectories are biased in exactly the way Pearl
predicts.

## What causal reasoning offers

Three concrete lifts.

**Do-calculus for off-policy evaluation.** Framing off-policy value
estimation as computing $\mathbb{E}[R \mid do(A = \pi(S))]$ makes the
identification assumptions explicit. When the observational data
comes from a policy that was *itself* confounded (e.g., a doctor
choosing treatment based on patient-severity information the agent
doesn't observe), standard OPE is biased and needs adjustment or
sensitivity analysis, not more data.

**Causal bandits.** A whole line of work (Bareinboim, Pearl) shows
that when you know part of the causal graph, you can beat standard
bandits — because you can rule out arms whose causal structure
implies they can't be optimal. The graph is a form of prior knowledge
that standard exploration ignores.

**Counterfactual policy improvement.** Given a trajectory produced by
policy $\pi_b$, you can ask: what would the trajectory have been under
$\pi$? Answering this uses rung-3 reasoning explicitly, and gives you
tighter policy-improvement guarantees than pure importance sampling
when the structural model is reliable.

## Where they still talk past each other

The two literatures use overlapping words for different things.
"State" in RL is a Markov sufficient statistic; "confounder" in
causal inference is an unobserved cause of both treatment and outcome
— sometimes these coincide, sometimes they don't. "Intervention" in
RL means "the agent acts"; in causal inference $do(x)$ means "fix
this variable to $x$ regardless of its usual parents", which is a
stronger statement.

The practical consequence: RL papers often *claim* to solve a causal
problem while implicitly assuming ignorability (no unobserved
confounders), and causal-inference papers often *claim* to enable
sequential decisions without engaging with credit assignment over long
horizons. The interesting research is where a paper takes both frames
seriously.

## Why it matters for real systems

Anywhere you're doing sequential decision-making from mostly-logged
data — clinical treatment policies, execution algorithms in trading,
recommendation systems learning from past logs — you are doing
causal RL whether you know it or not. Getting the causal frame right
turns a subtle bias into a diagnosable problem.
