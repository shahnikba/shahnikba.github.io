Bayesian posterior inference and any expectation over an intractable
distribution have the same shape: you can't compute the target
distribution's normalising constant, but you can evaluate it up to
that constant. **Markov chain Monte Carlo** methods generate samples
from such distributions by constructing a Markov chain whose
stationary distribution is the target.

## The setup

You have a distribution $\pi(x) \propto \tilde{\pi}(x)$ known only up
to a normalising constant $Z = \int \tilde{\pi}(x)\, dx$ that you
can't compute. You want either samples from $\pi$ or an expectation
$\mathbb{E}_\pi[f(x)]$ for some function $f$.

Every MCMC algorithm is a rule for stepping from $x_t$ to $x_{t+1}$
such that, if you run it long enough, the marginal distribution of
$x_t$ converges to $\pi$.

## Metropolis–Hastings — the general workhorse

The algorithm:

1. Propose $x^{*} \sim q(x^{*} \mid x_t)$ from some proposal
   distribution.
2. Compute the acceptance ratio
   $$
   \alpha = \min\left(1, \; \frac{\tilde{\pi}(x^{*})\, q(x_t \mid x^{*})}{\tilde{\pi}(x_t)\, q(x^{*} \mid x_t)}\right).
   $$
3. With probability $\alpha$, set $x_{t+1} = x^{*}$; otherwise
   $x_{t+1} = x_t$.

That's it. Under mild conditions the chain converges to $\pi$
regardless of the proposal, as long as the proposal is
"reachable" from any state.

The catch is how fast it converges. A proposal that's too narrow
means high acceptance but slow exploration. A proposal too wide
means most proposals are rejected. Tuning the proposal is the
practical difficulty.

## Gibbs sampling — the special case that pays off

When you can sample from the *full conditionals*
$\pi(x_i \mid x_{-i})$ analytically, **Gibbs sampling** cycles through
each dimension in turn, sampling one at a time. Every proposal is
accepted (it's a special case of Metropolis–Hastings with
probability 1). Widely used in models where conditional distributions
are conjugate — mixture models, LDA, hierarchical Bayesian setups.

Downside: correlated dimensions kill Gibbs. If $x_1$ and $x_2$ are
highly correlated, sampling them one at a time takes forever to
traverse the correlated ridge. Block Gibbs (sampling groups jointly)
helps but requires being able to sample from the block conditional.

## Hamiltonian Monte Carlo — using gradients

The reason HMC dominates modern Bayesian statistics: it uses gradient
information to make far-away proposals that get accepted with high
probability. The physics analogy: interpret the negative log
posterior as a potential energy, add a momentum variable, simulate
Hamiltonian dynamics, and use the endpoint as the proposal.

Because Hamiltonian dynamics conserves energy (approximately, given
finite-step-size integrators), the proposal moves far in state space
while approximately preserving the target density — giving high
acceptance probability at long distances. The result: HMC scales to
much higher dimensions than random-walk Metropolis before mixing time
becomes prohibitive.

**NUTS** (No-U-Turn Sampler) automatically tunes HMC's step count,
which was the main practical barrier. It's what Stan and PyMC use by
default, and it's the reason those probabilistic-programming systems
work.

## Where sampling loses

Sampling is expensive per usable draw. Modern deep-learning models
have millions of parameters; the correlated posterior over those
parameters is generally too high-dimensional and multi-modal for MCMC
to mix in useful wall-clock. Variational inference (approximating
the posterior with a tractable family) trades exactness for speed and
usually wins in these regimes.

The rule of thumb:

- **Bayesian model with a few dozen to a few hundred parameters** and
  well-defined posterior: HMC via Stan / PyMC / TFP. Gold standard for
  correctness.
- **High-dimensional posterior over neural-network weights**: MCMC is
  usually a dead end. Variational inference or stochastic-gradient MCMC
  (SGLD, HMC variants with noisy gradients) are the practical
  alternatives, both of which give you approximate answers.

## The diagnostics matter

Every MCMC user learns this the hard way: a chain that hasn't mixed
looks the same as a converged chain if you don't check. Standard
diagnostics — trace plots, $\hat{R}$ across multiple chains, effective
sample size — are non-negotiable. A chain reporting a beautiful
posterior mean while being stuck in one mode is worse than no answer,
because the answer looks trustworthy.
