A wearable that watches for falls has a data problem before it has a
modelling problem. Continuous accelerometry from a deployed cohort produces
something on the order of ten million two-second windows in a year; the
number of clinically confirmed falls in that same year is thirty. At a
positive rate near $10^{-6}$, [supervised
classification](/writing/learning-from-thirty-falls/) is not difficult so
much as ill-posed. Thirty examples cannot support a decision boundary over a
space that size, and every resampling trick that makes the training set look
balanced makes the deployment distribution look like a fiction.

The move that makes it tractable is to stop asking what a fall looks like.
Supervised detection needs positives. Density estimation asks what normal
looks like and whether a given window sits in the tail, and it needs only the
negatives, of which there are millions of hours. That exploits the exact
asymmetry in the data, and it buys something a classifier structurally cannot
have: generalisation to event types never observed. A model trained on thirty
forward falls onto hard floors learns forward falls onto hard floors. A
density over normal movement flags a backward fall onto carpet without having
seen one, because it was never asking about falls.

## Why the density model does not ship alone

It solves the label problem and creates a specificity problem. The model
flags everything unusual: a dropped handset, a car journey, an unusually
vigorous afternoon in the garden, a grandchild playing with the device, the
sensor left running on top of a washing machine. All genuinely improbable
under $p(x)$; none clinically meaningful. Precision collapses, and precision
is the product — a detector that cries wolf daily is ignored by carers inside
a week.

What ships is a cascade, and the cascade is what rescues the thirty labels.
Against ten million windows they were hopeless. Against a couple of hundred
pre-screened candidates the discrimination problem is four or five orders of
magnitude smaller, and thirty examples starts to be a workable training set —
particularly because the screen has already handed over the hard negatives,
the windows that look like falls and aren't. Random negatives teach a
classifier nothing. These teach it everything. The screen doubles as an
active-learning loop: it decides which two hundred windows are worth a
clinician's afternoon, which is how thirty labels become three hundred
without anyone reviewing a year of continuous signal.

## Point anomalies and drift are different problems

Falls are point anomalies — a single window, low likelihood, a threshold on
$\log p(x)$. Deterioration is not. Nothing is anomalous in any individual
window; activity level, sleep fragmentation and gait speed shift over weeks.
Per-window thresholding will never see it. That is a comparison of
$p_{\text{recent}}$ against $p_{\text{baseline}}$: a divergence problem, or
better, a sequential change-point one.

The natural instrument is CUSUM on the log-likelihood stream, accumulating
$S_t = \max(0,\, S_{t-1} + \ell_t - k)$ and alarming when it crosses $h$, with
$h$ tuned to a target average run length under the null. That gives a
false-alarm rate specified by design, denominated in patient-days between
alarms rather than in an abstract rate. It is the same machinery as
regime-change detection on a returns series, which is a pleasant thing to
notice if one has spent time on both.

## Conditioning, or you have built a clock

An unconditional density will flag vigorous movement at three in the morning.
That is wrong for a night-shift worker, and wrong for the patient who always
gets up at three, and it means a great deal of compute has been spent
building a circadian rhythm detector. What is wanted is
$p(x \mid \text{context})$: time of day, day of week, activity state, device
and wear position, and a patient embedding. The embedding is the one that
matters, because it gives a personalised baseline. A frail 85-year-old and an
active 65-year-old have quite different normal distributions, and a shared
threshold quietly trades sensitivity in one group against false alarms in the
other. That is an equity property as much as a statistical one.

## Stillness scores as maximally normal

Likelihood in high dimensions tracks complexity as much as familiarity.
Smooth, low-entropy inputs are assigned high density by most density models,
and in accelerometry the lowest-complexity signal available is stillness — a
handset lying on a table. Now recall the fall signature: freefall, impact,
then prolonged post-impact stillness. The single most alarming segment in the
record, an elderly person motionless on the floor for twenty minutes, is
exactly the segment a naive likelihood model calls normal. The blind spot
sits precisely where the need is greatest.

The mitigations are typicality tests rather than raw density — asking whether
$\log p(x)$ is near its expected value under the model, not merely high —
likelihood ratios against a background model, a learned feature space rather
than the raw signal, and modelling the transition structure rather than the
marginal. Mostly, though, the mitigation is knowing the trap is there. Most
people meet it in production.

## Evaluating on thirty events

Sensitivity cannot be estimated precisely from thirty positives and it is
better not to pretend otherwise. Fix the alert budget first — one alert per
patient-month, say — and report recall at that budget rather than ROC-AUC,
which integrates over operating points nobody would ever ship. Bootstrap a
confidence interval on the thirty and publish it; it will be wide, and saying
so is a credibility signal rather than a weakness. Split by subject, always:
gait is a biometric, and window-level splits leak identity into every number.
And track calibration over time, because normal drifts — seasonally, with
device upgrades, and as a cohort ages.
