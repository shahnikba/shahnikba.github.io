Gradient descent uses only first-order information — the local slope
of the loss. **Newton's method** uses the second-order Hessian too,
which lets it take fundamentally smarter steps. **Quasi-Newton**
methods approximate the Hessian at a fraction of the cost. Both
matter, both have limits.

## Newton's method

The update, derived from a local quadratic approximation:

$$
\theta_{t+1} \;=\; \theta_t - \left[\nabla^{2} f(\theta_t)\right]^{-1} \nabla f(\theta_t).
$$

Where gradient descent's step direction is just $-\nabla f$, Newton
pre-multiplies by the inverse Hessian. Geometrically it rescales the
gradient by curvature: dimensions with large curvature get small
steps, flat directions get large steps.

**Convergence.** Near a strict local minimum where the Hessian is
positive definite and Lipschitz, Newton converges *quadratically* —
the error at step $t+1$ scales as the square of the error at step
$t$. In practice this means once you're close, a handful of Newton
steps drives the error to machine precision.

**Cost.** Each step requires forming the Hessian ($O(n^2)$ storage,
$O(n^2)$ to $O(n^3)$ to compute) and inverting it ($O(n^3)$). For
$n = 10^6$ parameter neural networks, this is completely out of the
question. For $n = 100$ classical problems, Newton is often the right
choice.

## The quasi-Newton idea

If you can't afford the true Hessian, approximate it. **Quasi-Newton**
methods build an approximation $B_t \approx \nabla^{2} f(\theta_t)$
using only gradients, updating it after each step.

The core insight is the *secant condition*:

$$
B_{t+1} \, s_t = y_t, \qquad s_t = \theta_{t+1} - \theta_t, \qquad y_t = \nabla f(\theta_{t+1}) - \nabla f(\theta_t).
$$

This says: whatever the true Hessian is, the change in gradient over
the step is approximately the Hessian times the step. Any $B_{t+1}$
satisfying this is a plausible Hessian approximation.

## BFGS — the standard quasi-Newton

**BFGS** (Broyden–Fletcher–Goldfarb–Shanno) is the workhorse update
rule. It maintains $B_t^{-1}$ directly — avoiding the matrix inverse
at every step — via a rank-two update:

$$
B_{t+1}^{-1} = \left(I - \frac{s_t y_t^{T}}{y_t^{T} s_t}\right) B_t^{-1} \left(I - \frac{y_t s_t^{T}}{y_t^{T} s_t}\right) + \frac{s_t s_t^{T}}{y_t^{T} s_t}.
$$

It's a mouthful, but the properties are what matter: preserves
symmetry and positive-definiteness, satisfies the secant condition,
and converges super-linearly under standard assumptions.

## L-BFGS — when memory matters

Full BFGS stores an $n \times n$ inverse-Hessian approximation. For
large $n$, that's prohibitive. **L-BFGS** (Limited-memory BFGS)
maintains only the last $m$ pairs $(s_i, y_i)$ (typical $m = 5$ to
$20$) and reconstructs the effect of $B_t^{-1} \nabla f$ implicitly
via a two-loop recursion. Storage is $O(mn)$, cost per step is
$O(mn)$.

L-BFGS is the standard second-order method for medium-scale problems
(thousands to millions of parameters). It's the default optimiser in
scipy and R for a good reason.

## Where quasi-Newton loses to first-order methods

Two settings.

**Stochastic problems.** BFGS assumes the gradients are exact. In
mini-batch settings, the secant condition is corrupted by noise, and
the Hessian approximation degrades. Straightforward stochastic
variants exist (SGD + BFGS-style updates) but they don't beat well-
tuned SGD reliably. This is the topic of the stochastic quasi-Newton
note.

**Very large problems.** For $n \gg 10^{6}$, even L-BFGS's $O(mn)$
memory is expensive, and the sequential dependence in the two-loop
recursion doesn't parallelise as well as embarrassingly parallel
gradient computations. Modern deep-learning training uses first-order
methods almost exclusively for this reason.

## When to reach for Newton or quasi-Newton

Rule of thumb: use them when the problem is small enough that
$O(n^{2})$ storage is comfortable, the gradients are exact (batch
mode, not mini-batch), and the objective is smooth. Classical
scientific computing, calibration problems in quant finance,
maximum-likelihood fits for low-dimensional models — all natural
targets.

Use first-order methods when the problem is stochastic or huge, or
when convergence to machine precision doesn't matter (deep learning
is a good example: you want a solution that generalises, not one
that's exactly at the training-loss minimum).

The interesting middle ground is where the two ideas mix — natural
gradient, K-FAC, Sophia — that's the stochastic-quasi-Newton
territory.
