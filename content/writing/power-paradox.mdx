Celebrated UC Berkeley psychologist Dr. Dacher Keltner argues that
compassion and selflessness enable us to have the most influence over
others and the result is power as a force for good in the world.

That's the optimistic half of Keltner's thesis, and it's the one
worth starting with. The paradox comes from the second half — that
the same compassion and selflessness are exactly what people tend to
lose once the influence has arrived. Both halves are supported by
decades of experimental psychology. Neither one holds cleanly on its
own: read only the first and you get a warm story about how good
leaders rise; read only the second and you get another "power
corrupts" cliché.

The interesting question — for anyone actually leading a team — is
how to hold on to the traits that got you the influence in the first
place.

## The rise — how compassion buys influence

Keltner's positive finding is that low-power individuals who
consistently demonstrate empathy, generosity, and social intelligence
gain influence over their peers. Colleagues defer to them because
they feel *heard*, not because they're intimidated. This is the
"soft power" mechanism — durable, reciprocal, and often more
effective than positional authority.

In small teams this shows up clearly. The person who asks the
sharpest questions, listens to the answers, and shares credit tends
to end up shaping direction even without a title. Compassion is
strategy, not just character.

## The fall — the traits that got you there start eroding

The uncomfortable half of Keltner's work is the mirror-image finding.
Once someone has the influence, the traits that earned it tend to
weaken:

- **People with higher power become less accurate at reading others'
  emotions.** Brain scans of subjects told they're "the boss" of a
  temporary lab task show measurably reduced activity in the
  neural circuits that mirror others' expressions.
- **People with power take more resources for themselves** in
  dictator- and ultimatum-style games — including people who, an hour
  earlier as low-power participants, played generously.
- **People with power interrupt more, listen less, and break minor
  social rules** more often (eating others' food, ignoring polite
  conventions).

This isn't personality selection. Moving someone into a power
position causes the drift; it's not that already-callous people rise
faster. **Power changes the person**, and it changes them along the
axes that made them good in the first place.

## How this looks from inside a small technical team

Two structural features make it especially insidious in small teams:

**Deep expertise concentrates power invisibly.** A senior technical
leader who knows the codebase, the models, the market microstructure
better than anyone else has structural power even if the org chart
says flat. Junior colleagues subtly defer on every technical
decision. That deference is the same mechanism Keltner describes —
with none of the visible cues that would prompt a "boss" to check
themselves.

**Fast-cycling decisions amplify the effect.** In a trading team,
decisions get made minute-to-minute. There's no time for elaborate
consensus. Whoever has technical authority becomes the default
decider, and over time the structural role sculpts the person into
someone who reflexively decides rather than consults.

## Preserving the traits that earned the influence

None of these habits are original. All of them are deliberate — the
whole point is that they're the opposite of the drift.

**Ask more than you tell.** The literal ratio matters. If in a review
meeting the count is five statements per question, that's the wrong
side of the drift. Two or three questions per statement is closer to
what the flat-team version of you probably used to do.

**Solicit disagreement, and mean it.** *"What am I missing?"* only
elicits useful information when the asker has a track record of
actually listening to the answer. Building that track record takes
years; degrading it takes days.

**Track your own decision-vs.-consultation ratio.** In one job I
kept a rough tally for a month: what fraction of technical decisions
did I make alone versus in genuine consultation. The result surprised
me. It always does.

**Rotate authority on decision types you don't own.** If a junior
engineer is the technical lead on a specific service, defer to their
architectural decisions there even when you disagree — unless the
disagreement is important enough to warrant an explicit override
with reasoning. Overriding by default trains everyone to stop
proposing.

## The honest test

Keltner's book has a corollary the leadership-book category tends to
soften: the people most likely to notice the drift in themselves are
the least likely to be currently in power. Power reduces
self-awareness on exactly the dimensions where you'd want it
sharpened. External calibration — trusted colleagues who will tell
you when you're drifting — is the only reliable check.

Which is another way of saying: the honest test of whether you're
holding on to the compassion and selflessness that got you the
influence isn't your self-assessment. It's whether people who *could*
tell you still do.
