Dacher Keltner's *The Power Paradox* is filed under leadership
psychology, but the finding at its centre is closer to a physical law
of small organisations than to advice. The short version:

> **We gain power through empathy, generosity, and social intelligence;
> we lose those exact traits as soon as we have power.**

Reading this as a technical leader running small teams was
uncomfortable in the specific way that useful frameworks are.

## The mechanism

Keltner reviews decades of experimental social psychology showing
that:

- **People with higher power are less accurate at reading others'
  emotions.** A brain scan of someone told they're "the boss" of a
  temporary lab task shows measurably reduced activity in the neural
  circuits that mirror others' expressions.
- **People with power take more resources for themselves in dictator-
  and ultimatum-style games** — including people who, an hour earlier
  as low-power players, were generous.
- **People with power interrupt more, listen less, and are more
  likely to break minor social rules** (eating others' food, ignoring
  polite conventions).

The finding survives across contexts (workplace, family, one-off lab
manipulations) and doesn't seem to be personality-selected — moving
someone into a power position causes the drift; it's not that
already-callous people rise. Power *changes* the person.

## Why it applies to technical teams

Two things make small technical teams particularly susceptible:

**Deep expertise concentrates power invisibly.** A senior technical
leader who knows the codebase, the models, the market microstructure
better than anyone else on the team has structural power even if the
org chart says flat. Junior colleagues are subtly deferring to that
expertise on every technical decision. That deference is the same
mechanism that produces the empathy drift Keltner describes — with
none of the visible cues that would prompt a "boss" to check
themselves.

**Fast-cycling decisions amplify the effect.** In a trading team,
decisions get made minute-to-minute, hour-to-hour. There's no time
for elaborate consensus. Whoever has technical authority becomes the
default decider. Over months and years, that structural role sculpts
the person into someone who reflexively decides rather than
consults.

## Countermeasures I've watched work

None of these are original. All of them require deliberate practice
because they're the opposite of the drift.

**Ask more than you tell.** The literal ratio matters. If in a
review meeting I make five statements per question I ask, I'm on the
wrong side of the drift. Two-to-three questions per statement is
closer to what the flat-team version of me used to do.

**Solicit disagreement explicitly.** *"What am I missing?"* only
elicits useful information when the asker has a track record of
actually listening to the answer. Building that track record takes
years; degrading it takes days.

**Track your own decision-vs.-consultation ratio.** In one job I
kept a rough tally for a month: what fraction of technical decisions
did I make alone versus in genuine consultation. The result surprised
me. It always does.

**Rotate authority on decision types you don't own.** If a junior
engineer is the technical lead on a specific service, defer to their
architectural decisions there even when you disagree — unless the
disagreement is important enough to warrant an explicit override
with reasoning. Overriding by default trains everyone to stop
proposing.

## The uncomfortable corollary

Keltner's book has a corollary the leadership-book category tends to
soften: **the people most likely to notice this drift in themselves
are the least likely to be currently in power**. Power reduces
self-awareness on exactly the dimensions where you'd want it
sharpened. External calibration — trusted colleagues who will tell
you when you're drifting — becomes the only reliable check.

Which is another way of saying: the honest test of whether you're
falling into the paradox isn't your self-assessment. It's whether
people who could tell you still do.
