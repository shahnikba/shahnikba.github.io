A stock whose volatility is itself random is not a Markov process in its own
price. Under Heston the state is the pair $(S_t,V_t)$; knowing today's price
tells you nothing about how fast the price is currently moving, and the
increment over the next instant depends on a coordinate you have not written
down. The same is true of any model in which the diffusion coefficient is
driven by its own noise, and of models where the drift looks back over the
whole history.

A vanilla European option, meanwhile, depends on nothing but the law of $S_T$
at a single instant. The gap between what the model carries and what the payoff
needs is the opening Gyöngy's theorem exploits.

## The projection

Take a process with an Itô differential,

$$
dX_t = b_t\,dt + \sigma_t\,dW_t,
$$

where $b$ and $\sigma$ are adapted to the full filtration and may depend on the
entire past. Gyöngy proved that there exists a Markov diffusion

$$
dY_t = \hat b(t,Y_t)\,dt + \hat\sigma(t,Y_t)\,dW_t
$$

with $Y_t \stackrel{\text{law}}{=} X_t$ for every fixed $t$, and that its
coefficients are conditional expectations of the originals:

$$
\hat b(t,x) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[b_t \mid X_t = x\right],
\qquad
\hat a(t,x) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\sigma_t\sigma_t^{\!\top} \mid X_t = x\right],
$$

with $\hat a = \hat\sigma\hat\sigma^{\!\top}$. That conditioning is the whole
content of the result. The projected coefficient at the point $x$ is the
average of the true coefficient over every path that happens to be at $x$ at
time $t$, weighted by how likely those paths are. Everything the original
process knew and the projection does not — the current level of variance, the
history of the drift — is integrated out against that conditional law.

Only the marginals coincide. The two processes do not share paths, and nothing
about the theorem suggests they should.

## Local volatility as the projection of everything

Applied to $dS_t = rS_t\,dt + \sigma_t S_t\,dW_t$, the projection is a
diffusion whose diffusion coefficient depends on time and spot alone,

$$
\sigma_{\text{loc}}^2(t,S) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\sigma_t^2 \mid S_t = S\right],
$$

which is the local volatility function. Under Heston this reads
$\mathbb{E}[V_t \mid S_t = S]$ — emphatically not $\mathbb{E}[V_t]$. The
conditioning is what makes the projected coefficient move with $S$ at all, and
therefore what produces skew in a model that has no stochastic volatility left
in it. Heston, [SABR](/writing/sabr-model/) and their mixtures all project onto
local volatility models; they differ in the leverage they generate, not in the
kind of object they collapse to.

Because a call payoff is a function of $S_T$ alone, equality of laws is
equality of prices: the projected model reproduces the entire vanilla surface,
strike by strike and maturity by maturity, of whatever complicated thing it was
projected from. Since it is a one-factor Markov diffusion it is also far
cheaper to calibrate and simulate.

## What a marginal cannot carry

The theorem is silent about the joint law of $(S_{t_1},S_{t_2})$. Two processes
with identical marginals at every instant can differ entirely in how they get
from one instant to the next, and every payoff that reads the path is exposed
to that difference: barriers, cliquets, autocallables, lookbacks, realised
variance products, Asians in general. This is the precise reason local
volatility calibrates beautifully to vanillas and then misprices the exotics
that were the reason for building a model in the first place. The failure is
not a defect of the calibration; it is the theorem telling you what it never
promised.

## Reading Dupire backwards

Dupire begins at the other end, from a quoted surface $C(K,T)$, and
differentiates it into a local volatility. Gyöngy's theorem is what entitles
him to expect an answer: if the quotes are generated by some arbitrage-free
Itô diffusion, a Markov diffusion matching its one-dimensional laws exists, and
Dupire's construction is a constructive realisation of that projection.

It also frames the calibration problem for models that keep both objects. If
one writes stochastic volatility multiplied by a leverage function and demands
that the projection of the combined dynamics equal the Dupire surface, the
condition to be solved is again a conditional expectation, taken under the
law of the very process one is trying to build. That circularity — not the
projection itself — is where the difficulty in local stochastic volatility
lives.
