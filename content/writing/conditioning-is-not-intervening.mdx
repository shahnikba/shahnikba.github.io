Reinforcement learning has a probabilistic reading that is now standard. Write
the trajectory as a graphical model — latent states, actions, a prior policy —
and attach to each step a binary optimality variable with likelihood
$p(\mathcal{O}_t = 1 \mid s_t, a_t) \propto \exp r(s_t, a_t)$. Condition on
optimality throughout, lower-bound the log evidence, and the maximum-entropy
objective falls out, with soft value functions appearing as log-partition
functions. Control becomes inference, and the whole apparatus of variational
approximation becomes available for policy learning.

The formulation has a known defect, and the defect is more interesting than the
formulation. Exact inference on the optimality-conditioned graph gives the wrong
answer under stochastic dynamics. Conditioning on a good outcome tilts the
posterior over transitions, so that

$$
p(s_{t+1} \mid s_t, a_t, \mathcal{O}_{1:T}) \;\neq\; p(s_{t+1} \mid s_t, a_t),
$$

and the resulting policy behaves as though it can influence chance. It buys the
lottery ticket, because in the posterior the ticket wins.

## The repair has a name it is not given

The standard remedy is to restrict the variational family: pin the dynamics
factor to its true value and let only $q(a_t \mid s_t)$ vary. Stated that way it
sounds like a technical convenience, a choice of approximating family made for
tractability. It is not. Fixing the mechanism $P(s_{t+1} \mid s_t, a_t)$ while
severing whatever determined the action is graph mutilation — it is
$\mathrm{do}(a_t)$, reached from the inference side and given a different name.
The optimism bug is the observational–interventional confusion in its purest
form, and the structured variational posterior is the intervention that resolves
it.

That correspondence is worth stating plainly because it makes the two
literatures one. Maximum-entropy RL is not merely compatible with causal
inference; its central correctness condition is a causal condition that the
derivation obtains without ever writing a $\mathrm{do}$.

## Where pinning the dynamics stops being enough

The repair handles the tilt, but it assumes the logged behaviour policy is a
function of recorded state. In any real deployment log it is not. The clinician,
or the app, or the trader acted partly on something absent from the schema, and
the moment that is true, $P(y \mid a)$ and $P(y \mid \mathrm{do}(a))$ come apart
in the data itself, before any inference procedure touches it.

This is bad for the variational formulation in a specific and slightly cruel
way. The term that makes offline policy learning safe is the divergence penalty
$-\mathrm{KL}(q \,\|\, \pi_{\text{behaviour}})$, which anchors the learnt policy
to what was actually done. If what was actually done is confounded, conservatism
inherits the bias rather than correcting it, and the more cautious the agent, the
more faithfully it reproduces the confounding. Bareinboim's confounded bandits
make the point at minimum size: the arm that looks best observationally can be
strictly worse under intervention, and an agent regularised towards the log will
never find out.

## Bounds in place of tuned pessimism

With unobserved confounders the interventional value of a policy is
set-identified rather than point-identified. That reads as an obstacle and is
better treated as an ingredient. Constrain the variational problem so that
improvement is accepted only when the *lower* bound on the interventional value
exceeds the behaviour policy's value, and offline conservatism stops being a
hyperparameter someone tuned until the deployment stopped failing. It becomes a
consequence of what the data can and cannot identify — the same quantity,
derived rather than chosen.

## The counterfactual question is the one being asked

Interventional reasoning answers what happens to a population under a policy.
Clinical review asks something narrower: what would have happened to *this*
patient, on this trajectory, had the alert fired an hour earlier. Given a
structural model with exogenous noise $U$, that question is answerable —
condition on the observed trajectory to obtain a posterior over $U$, then
re-evaluate the alternative action against that same draw. Inference over $U$
is a variational problem of precisely the kind the
[control-as-inference](/writing/rl-as-probabilistic-modelling/) formulation
already solves, so nothing new is required except the willingness to write the
structural model down.

It also closes a loop with an older problem. Training a detector on simulated
events and deploying it on real ones was framed, at the time, as domain
adaptation — a transfer gap to be shrunk. Transportability is the same question
asked causally, and it comes with conditions under which a policy learnt in one
population remains valid in another, rather than a hope that the gap is small.
