Pearl's causal ladder has three rungs:

1. **Association** — $P(y \mid x)$. What we see when we observe $x$.
2. **Intervention** — $P(y \mid do(x))$. What we see when we *set* $x$.
3. **Counterfactuals** — $P(y_{x} \mid x', y')$. What *would have*
   happened to unit $i$ under treatment $x$, given that we actually
   observed $x'$ and $y'$ for that unit.

Rung 1 you can do with regression. Rung 2 you can do with a randomised
controlled trial — or with careful adjustment for confounders, given a
causal graph. Rung 3 requires more.

## Why the third rung is different

Rungs 1 and 2 are statements about **populations**. Rung 3 is a
statement about a **specific unit**, in a world that didn't happen.

That's not a small distinction. If I run an RCT on 10,000 patients and
find that drug A extends life by 3 years on average, I've learned
something about the population (rung 2). If a specific patient took
drug A and died at 60, and someone asks *"would this patient still be
alive if they'd taken drug B?"* — that's a counterfactual (rung 3),
and no amount of population averages answers it.

## The mechanics

Formally, a counterfactual query needs a **structural causal model** —
a set of equations, one per endogenous variable, mapping parents (and
noise) to that variable:

$$
Y \;=\; f_Y(X, U_Y), \qquad X \;=\; f_X(U_X).
$$

The recipe (Pearl calls it *abduction–action–prediction*):

1. **Abduction.** Use the observed evidence $(x', y')$ to update the
   distribution over the exogenous noise $U$ for this unit. The noise
   captures everything unit-specific we didn't measure.
2. **Action.** Modify the model to fix $X = x$ — the counterfactual
   treatment — replacing $f_X$ with the constant $x$. This is a
   $do$-operation on the modified model.
3. **Prediction.** Use the modified model, with the updated noise
   distribution from step 1, to compute the distribution of $Y$ under
   the counterfactual $X = x$.

The key move is step 1 — the actual observation constrains what
unmeasured causes were plausibly at work for *this specific unit*.

## Why RCTs don't give you counterfactuals

A common confusion: "we ran an RCT, so we have the counterfactual."
No — an RCT gives you the *expected* counterfactual outcome under
each treatment arm. It tells you the difference in population means.
It doesn't tell you what any *individual* patient would have
experienced under the other arm. That distinction matters for
personalised medicine, personalised policy, and — always — for
retrospective what-if questions.

## When they're identifiable, and when they're not

Rung-3 queries are the least identifiable rung: even with a
randomised experiment, some counterfactual quantities are
non-identifiable from data alone and require assumptions about the
functional form of the structural equations. This is the price of
asking the hardest question.

Practically, three families of counterfactual quantities show up:

- **Effect of Treatment on the Treated (ETT).** Among those who
  actually received treatment, what would have happened without it?
  Identifiable under standard conditional-exchangeability assumptions.
- **Probability of Necessity (PN).** Given the outcome happened,
  what's the probability it *wouldn't* have happened without the
  treatment? Often identifiable, but sensitive to monotonicity
  assumptions.
- **Probability of Sufficiency (PS).** Given the outcome didn't
  happen, what's the probability the treatment *would* have caused it?
  Companion to PN; same monotonicity story.

## Why I keep coming back to it

Every practical question of the form *"was this decision the right
one?"* — in trading, in policy, in medicine — is a counterfactual
question. Even when we can't get a clean identifiable answer, being
explicit about which counterfactual we're asking about clarifies what
data or assumption would move the estimate. That's usually more
valuable than the estimate itself.
