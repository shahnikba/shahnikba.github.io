Almost every model training loop is some flavour of gradient descent.
It's the workhorse. Understanding why the simple version works — and
where it breaks — is the price of entry for everything else.

## The update

Given a differentiable objective $f: \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$,
gradient descent iterates

$$
\theta_{t+1} \;=\; \theta_t - \eta \, \nabla f(\theta_t)
$$

where $\eta > 0$ is the step size (learning rate). The intuition: move
in the direction of steepest descent, controlled by $\eta$.

## Why it converges (when it does)

For convex $f$ with $L$-Lipschitz gradient, a fixed step size
$\eta = 1/L$ gives a convergence rate of

$$
f(\theta_t) - f(\theta^{*}) \;\leq\; \frac{L \|\theta_0 - \theta^{*}\|^{2}}{2t}.
$$

That's $O(1/t)$ — sub-linear but reliable. For strongly convex $f$
with condition number $\kappa = L/\mu$, the rate improves to linear:

$$
\|\theta_t - \theta^{*}\|^{2} \;\leq\; \left(1 - \frac{1}{\kappa}\right)^{t} \|\theta_0 - \theta^{*}\|^{2}.
$$

The condition number $\kappa$ is the ratio of the largest to smallest
Hessian eigenvalue — it measures how "elongated" the loss landscape
is. High $\kappa$ means slow convergence, and this is where the
elaborate methods start earning their keep.

## The step-size problem

The theory gives you $\eta = 1/L$, but you rarely know $L$ in
practice. Too large a step causes divergence; too small wastes
iterations. Three practical strategies:

- **Line search.** At each step, search for an $\eta$ that gives
  sufficient decrease (Armijo/Wolfe conditions). Robust but expensive
  per step.
- **Decay schedules.** Start with a large $\eta$, shrink it over
  training (e.g., $\eta_t = \eta_0 / (1 + \alpha t)$ or step decays at
  milestones). Cheap and dominant in deep learning practice.
- **Adaptive per-parameter rates.** AdaGrad, RMSProp, Adam. Different
  parameters get different effective step sizes based on their gradient
  history.

## Where the simple version breaks

Two failure modes matter in practice.

**Ill-conditioning.** For elongated loss surfaces (very different
eigenvalues along different directions), gradient descent zig-zags
across the narrow valley and inches down the long axis. Slow. The
answer is either a preconditioner (multiply the gradient by
$H^{-1}$-ish matrices — Newton, quasi-Newton) or an adaptive method
that approximates this per-dimension.

**Non-convexity.** In neural networks the loss surface is
non-convex; gradient descent converges to a local minimum, saddle
point, or high-loss region depending on initialisation and step size.
Empirically, the local minima found by SGD in overparameterised
networks tend to generalise well — but this is empirical, not theory.
Saddle points were feared for a while but turn out to be escaped
easily by any method with a bit of noise (SGD does this for free).

## Momentum — the cheapest useful upgrade

The next natural step: accumulate a velocity vector.

$$
v_{t+1} = \beta v_t + \nabla f(\theta_t), \qquad \theta_{t+1} = \theta_t - \eta \, v_{t+1}.
$$

Typical $\beta = 0.9$. Momentum smooths out zig-zagging in narrow
valleys and speeds convergence on ill-conditioned problems.
Practically, plain SGD with momentum is often within striking distance
of adaptive methods on well-tuned deep-learning problems — and easier
to reason about.

**Nesterov's variant** evaluates the gradient at the
"look-ahead" point $\theta_t - \eta \beta v_t$ rather than
$\theta_t$. Slightly better theoretical rates for smooth convex
problems and often a small practical improvement.

## Why it's still the default

For all the elaborate optimisers in the literature, gradient descent
(with momentum, with a schedule) remains the dominant tool for
large-scale problems for one boring reason: **cheap iterations**. Each
step needs only a gradient — no matrix inverses, no factorisations. On
modern hardware you can afford far more steps than you can afford
Hessian evaluations, and often that trade wins even when the per-step
progress is worse.

The lesson: understand what makes gradient descent slow (ill-conditioning)
and reach for the fancy methods when that's actually the bottleneck.
Otherwise, GD with a well-tuned schedule is hard to beat.
