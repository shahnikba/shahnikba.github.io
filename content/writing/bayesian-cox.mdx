Two tools that get taught apart and used together. [Cox](/writing/cox-proportional-hazards/)
gives you a hazard ratio without ever committing to a shape for the baseline
risk; MCMC gives you a posterior when the integral in Bayes' rule refuses to
close. Neither needs the other. But the survival problems worth the trouble
— sparse subgroups, covariates measured through a proxy, a decision waiting
at the end — are the ones where fitting them together earns its cost.

## The ranking argument inside Cox

Cox models the hazard: the instantaneous risk of the event at time $t$,
conditional on having survived to $t$.

$$h(t \mid x) = h_0(t)\,\exp(\beta^\top x)$$

The move that made it famous is leaving $h_0(t)$ unspecified. It is never
estimated. Cox showed that $\beta$ can be recovered from the partial
likelihood, which at each observed event asks only why *this* member of the
risk set failed rather than one of the others still in it.

$$L(\beta) = \prod_{i \,:\, \delta_i = 1} \frac{\exp(\beta^\top x_i)}{\sum_{j \in R(t_i)} \exp(\beta^\top x_j)}$$

That is a ranking statement. It depends on the order of events, not their
spacing, so the baseline cancels — parametric in the covariates,
non-parametric in time. What comes out the other side is $\exp(\beta_k)$,
the hazard ratio per unit of covariate $k$; in the sleep-onset work, the
risk relative to the late-evening reference window. The price is
proportionality, hazard ratios held constant over time, which is what
Schoenfeld residuals exist to interrogate.

## Sampling because the integral will not close

MCMC answers a different question. Given a posterior

$$p(\theta \mid D) = \frac{p(D \mid \theta)\,p(\theta)}{\int p(D \mid \theta)\,p(\theta)\,d\theta}$$

the denominator is intractable in anything past a toy dimension. So you
stop trying to write the posterior down and draw samples from it instead;
any quantity you actually wanted — a mean, a credible interval, a tail
probability — becomes a summary of the draws.

The mechanism is a Markov chain whose stationary distribution is the
posterior. Metropolis–Hastings does it in two lines: propose
$\theta' \sim q(\cdot \mid \theta)$, accept with probability

$$\alpha = \min\left(1, \frac{p(D \mid \theta')\,p(\theta')}{p(D \mid \theta)\,p(\theta)}\right)$$

The normalising constant cancels in that ratio, and that cancellation is
the entire trick. In code the whole accept step is four lines, worked in
logs:

```
proposal = current + rng.normal(scale=PROPOSAL_SD)
proposal_lp = log_posterior(proposal, x, t, event)
if np.log(rng.uniform()) < proposal_lp - current_lp:
    current, current_lp = proposal, proposal_lp
```

Everything the diagnostics care about is legible in that picture. Started
deliberately at −1.2, well below the mass, the chain spends a few hundred
iterations climbing before it settles — that early stretch is burn-in and
gets thrown away. After it, the posterior mean is 0.794 with a 95%
credible interval of (0.662, 0.925), which is a fair recovery of the truth.
But of the 5,250 draws retained, the effective sample size is about 1,262.
The chain is autocorrelated by construction, each state a small step from
the last, so the nominal count flatters you by roughly a factor of four.
That gap is what ESS reports and, across chains, what R-hat is watching.

It is also the argument for better proposals. Modern practice uses
Hamiltonian Monte Carlo and NUTS — Stan, PyMC, NumPyro — where the gradient
of the log-posterior buys long, informed moves in place of the blind random
walk above, and the same wall-clock budget returns far more independent
information.

## Where the two meet

Standard Cox is frequentist: maximise the partial likelihood, invert the
Hessian, read off standard errors. Going Bayesian means putting priors on
$\beta$ and treating the partial likelihood as though it were a likelihood.
That is not a fudge — it is a valid partial likelihood, and Sinha, Ibrahim
and Chen (2003) give the argument — so you can sample
$p(\beta \mid D) \propto L_{\text{partial}}(\beta)\,\pi(\beta)$ directly.
The alternative is to stop hiding $h_0(t)$ and model it: piecewise-constant
hazard, a gamma process, a B-spline log-hazard, then sample the joint
posterior over $(\beta, h_0)$. That is what `brms`, `stan_surv` and any
hand-rolled Stan model are doing.

## When the posterior is worth the wait

Sparse subgroups are the clearest case: a hierarchical prior shrinks
small-cell estimates towards the group mean instead of reporting an
implausible hazard ratio with an interval wide enough to drive through.
Non-proportionality is the next — treating $\beta(t)$ as a smooth function
needs a prior on smoothness, and the resulting posterior has no closed
form. Frailty and site-level clustering integrate out cleanly by sampling.
Competing risks give you a joint posterior over cause-specific hazards, and
with it coherent uncertainty on the cumulative incidence functions.
Measurement error matters more than it looks: accelerometer-derived sleep
onset is a proxy, and a measurement-error layer propagates that into the
hazard ratio rather than pretending the covariate is exact.

The row that usually decides it, though, is decision-facing output. What
you want is $P(\text{HR} > 1 \mid D)$, not a p-value, and that is read
straight off the draws. Anything downstream — expected utility of an
intervention, a threshold on an alerting rule — becomes a transformation of
the samples rather than a delta-method approximation.

The bill arrives in compute. A Cox fit on a cohort of a hundred thousand
runs in seconds; the Bayesian version, recomputing the risk-set denominator
at every event time on every iteration, runs for hours, because that
denominator is $O(n)$. The usual escape is a piecewise-exponential
reformulation as a Poisson regression, which HMC finds far more agreeable.
