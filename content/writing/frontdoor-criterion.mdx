The backdoor criterion is the easy case: block all the spurious
paths from $X$ to $Y$ by conditioning on observed confounders.
Sometimes you cannot — the confounder is unmeasured. The **frontdoor
criterion** is a subtler tool that identifies the causal effect
anyway, using an intermediate variable that lies on the causal path.

## The setup

Consider the following DAG. $X$ affects $Y$, but there's an
unobserved confounder $U$ that also causes both:

$$
X \rightarrow M \rightarrow Y, \qquad U \rightarrow X, \qquad U \rightarrow Y.
$$

The backdoor path $X \leftarrow U \rightarrow Y$ is unblockable — $U$
is not observed. But if there's an observed **mediator** $M$ that
fully mediates the effect of $X$ on $Y$, we can still identify the
causal effect.

## The three conditions

$M$ satisfies the frontdoor criterion relative to $(X, Y)$ if:

1. **$M$ intercepts every directed path from $X$ to $Y$** — i.e., $M$
   is a full mediator; there's no direct arrow from $X$ to $Y$ that
   bypasses $M$.
2. **No unblocked backdoor path from $X$ to $M$** — usually
   because $M$ has no confounding with $X$ (nothing else causes $M$
   from the same source as $X$).
3. **All backdoor paths from $M$ to $Y$ are blocked by $X$** — $X$
   is a sufficient adjustment set for the $M$-to-$Y$ leg.

## The frontdoor formula

If those hold, the causal effect is identified by a two-step formula:

$$
P(y \mid do(x)) \;=\; \sum_{m} P(m \mid x) \sum_{x'} P(y \mid m, x')\, P(x').
$$

The intuition: compute the effect of $X$ on $M$ (the first sum),
then the effect of $M$ on $Y$ adjusted for $X$ (the inner sum), then
compose them.

The remarkable part: we never needed to observe or condition on $U$.
The mediator does the work.

## The canonical example — smoking, tar, cancer

Pearl's original example, though tobacco companies would dispute it.
Suppose:

- $X$ = smoking
- $M$ = tar deposits in lungs
- $Y$ = lung cancer
- $U$ = an unobserved "genetic disposition" that causes both smoking
  behaviour and cancer independently of tar

If tar fully mediates the effect of smoking on cancer, and if the
genetic disposition doesn't independently affect tar deposits, and
if smoking blocks the backdoor from tar to cancer, then we can
identify the causal effect of smoking on cancer from observational
data — even without observing genetics — using the frontdoor formula.

Whether these assumptions actually hold is a scientific question, not
a statistical one. That's the whole game.

## Why it feels magical (and why it isn't)

The frontdoor criterion identifies a causal effect without observing
or adjusting for the confounder. Read hastily, that sounds impossible
— surely you need to account for the confounder somehow?

The trick is that the mediator gives you two clean sub-problems: $X$
affects $M$ with no confounding, and $M$ affects $Y$ with only $X$ as
the confounder. Composing them isolates the causal path and cancels
the confounding. The unobserved $U$ never enters because its influence
on $Y$ is captured by conditioning on the observed $X$ in the second
step.

## Where it matters in practice

Frontdoor applications are rarer than backdoor applications in the
wild because the mediator conditions are strict — you need to
plausibly claim that the observed $M$ captures the *entire* effect of
$X$ on $Y$. In experimental sciences (mediation analysis in
biology, mechanistic pharmacology) this can hold. In social science
and financial applications it usually doesn't; treatment effects
almost always have direct as well as mediated components.

The frontdoor criterion is worth knowing anyway. Even when it doesn't
apply, thinking about *what would be enough* often turns up nearby
strategies — sensitivity analysis for the direct-effect assumption,
partial identification bounds, or an instrumental-variable design
built around the same mediator.
