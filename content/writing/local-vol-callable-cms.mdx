Local volatility has a seductive property: calibrated to the market,
it reprices every European vanilla exactly. Given a full surface of
option prices, Dupire hands you a diffusion

$$
dS_t = \sigma_{\text{loc}}(S_t, t)\, S_t \, dW_t
$$

whose terminal marginals match the observed smile at every strike and
maturity. If the thing you are pricing is a function of $S_T$ alone,
you are done — the marginal is all the payoff can see.

Gatheral's criticism is that almost nothing interesting is a function
of $S_T$ alone. The moment a payoff depends on the *path*, or on a
decision taken at intermediate dates, matching the marginals stops
being enough. Local vol fits $\mathbb{P}(S_T)$ at every date and still
gets the *forward* dynamics — how volatility behaves conditional on
where the rate has travelled — quantitatively wrong.

A callable CMS is the cleanest example I know, and small enough to
put a number on.

## The experiment

Take one stochastic-volatility model as the truth:

$$
dS_t = \sigma_t S_t\, dW_t, \qquad
d\sigma_t = \kappa(\theta - \sigma_t)\,dt + \xi\, dZ_t, \qquad
dW\,dZ = \rho\, dt.
$$

Now build a local-vol model that agrees with it on every European
price, and ask what the two disagree about. The local variance that
reproduces the marginals is the Dupire local volatility, and the
cleanest way to write it is as the conditional expectation of
instantaneous variance given the level,

$$
\sigma_{\text{loc}}^2(S,t) = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\sigma_t^2 \mid S_t = S\right].
$$

This is the same surface Dupire's price-differentiation formula
targets; here we read it straight off the simulated paths by binning
$\sigma^2$ against $S$ at each date. By construction it reproduces the
terminal marginal at every date, so every European reprices.

The [Monte Carlo](https://github.com/shahnikba/shahnikba.github.io/blob/main/code/local-vol-callable-cms/callable_cms.c)
simulates the SV model, calibrates that surface, and reprices under
it. The product: a note paying the CMS rate twice a year, which the
issuer may redeem once the rate breaches a barrier just above the
money — a payoff whose life is a stopping time, so its value depends on
the joint law $\mathbb{P}(S_{T_1},S_{T_2},\dots)$, not on any single
marginal.

## What comes out

Both models are drift-free by construction; the Monte Carlo confirms
this at the end of the run — $\mathbb{E}[S_T] = 0.02998$ under SV and
$0.03000$ under LV, against $S_0 = 3.00\%$ — so no simulation bias
contaminates the comparisons that follow.

The European smile is then reproduced to Monte-Carlo accuracy across
the full strike range, which is the whole point of the calibration:

```
European call at T=5y      SV          LV        err
   0.020               0.011487    0.011514   +0.2%
   0.025               0.008438    0.008464   +0.3%
   0.030               0.006269    0.006292   +0.4%
   0.035               0.004771    0.004790   +0.4%
   0.040               0.003729    0.003745   +0.4%
```

Now the callable. Split it into the coupon strip (never called) and
the issuer's embedded call — the piece the client sells:

```
                        SV(truth)   LV
Coupon strip (call off)  0.149938   0.150026
Callable CMS note        0.082153   0.078880
Embedded issuer call     0.067785   0.071146   +5.0%
```

The coupon strip is identical — it only ever sees the marginals, and
is in fact worth $\sum_i \mathbb{E}[S_{T_i}]$ under either model. The
embedded call is not. Priced under a local-vol model that agrees with
the SV world on every vanilla, the issuer's option is worth about
**5% more** than under the SV dynamics themselves. Same marginals,
different call value — because the call decision at each date depends
on the volatility state, and local vol has collapsed that state onto
the level.

## Why local vol can't see it

Follow a path to some intermediate date and suppose the rate sits at
3%. The local-vol model reads that number and knows everything it is
allowed to know: the level is 3%, so the volatility from here is
$\sigma_{\text{loc}}(3\%, t)$, full stop. The SV model looks at the
same 3% and sees two worlds — a calm one where the barrier is far, and
a stressed one where the same 3% is one move from being called. Both
carry $S_t = 3\%$; they carry different

$$
\mathbb{P}\!\left(\text{called before }T \mid S_t = 3\%\right),
$$

and that is what sets the value of the call. The marginals cannot
tell them apart.

## What a desk actually does

The toy keeps the two things local vol conflates — the level and the
volatility state — separate only long enough to make the point. A
production callable-CMS system keeps them separate throughout. Rate
dynamics come from a term-structure model, Hull–White or an LMM/BGM
forward-rate setup, so the curve evolves rather than a single rate.
The volatility state gets its own dynamics, typically
[SABR](/writing/sabr-model/). And the call is an optimal-stopping
problem,

$$
V_t = \max\big(\text{continue},\ \text{exercise}\big),
$$

solved on a tree, a PDE, or by Longstaff–Schwartz regression. All of
that exists because a callable CMS never asks $\mathbb{P}(S_T)$. It
asks about the joint law of $(S_{T_1}, S_{T_2}, \dots, S_T)$ — and
answering that honestly needs a model of how volatility behaves along
the way, not just a surface that reprices today's vanillas.
