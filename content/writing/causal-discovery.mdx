Standard causal inference starts with a DAG and asks *"given this
graph, what can I identify?"* **Causal discovery** flips the question:
*"given data, can I recover the graph?"*

The answer is a qualified yes — under assumptions that are worth being
explicit about, because they're the whole game.

## The three assumption pillars

Every discovery algorithm rests on some combination of:

1. **Causal sufficiency.** All common causes of any two observed
   variables are themselves observed. In other words: no hidden
   confounders. This is the strong assumption almost no observational
   dataset actually satisfies.
2. **The causal Markov condition.** A variable is independent of its
   non-descendants given its parents. This is what makes the graph
   testable from data — it implies conditional independences you can
   check.
3. **Faithfulness.** All conditional independences in the data arise
   from the graph structure, not from cancellations of specific
   parameter values. Faithfulness rules out coincidences where two
   effects cancel out exactly.

Under all three, the graph implies a testable set of conditional
independences, and any two graphs implying the same set are
indistinguishable from data alone (they form a *Markov equivalence
class*).

## The PC algorithm — constraint-based

The classic algorithm is **PC** (Peter Spirtes, Clark Glymour):

1. Start with a complete undirected graph.
2. For each pair $(X, Y)$, test conditional independence given
   subsets of the neighbours. Remove edges where independence holds.
3. Orient the surviving edges using colliders — if $X - Z - Y$ has
   $X \perp Y$ unconditionally but $X \not\perp Y \mid Z$, then $Z$
   is a collider ($X \rightarrow Z \leftarrow Y$).
4. Propagate remaining orientations by rules that avoid creating new
   colliders or cycles.

The output is the *CPDAG* (completed partially-directed acyclic
graph) representing the Markov equivalence class. Some edges have a
direction, others don't — the latter are genuinely unidentifiable from
data alone under the three assumptions.

## Score-based approaches: GES and beyond

An alternative family fits a score (BIC, penalised likelihood) over
graph structures. **GES** (greedy equivalence search) moves through
the space of Markov equivalence classes, adding then pruning edges.
It's typically more stable than PC in finite samples because it uses
all the data at once rather than a sequence of independence tests.

Both approaches share the same fundamental identifiability limit: they
return an equivalence class, not a unique graph.

## LiNGAM — non-Gaussianity breaks the tie

**LiNGAM** (Shimizu et al.) is the surprising result. If the data
follow a linear structural model with non-Gaussian noise, the causal
graph is *uniquely* identifiable — not just up to equivalence class.
The magic is that under Gaussianity, all model directions produce the
same joint distribution, but under non-Gaussianity they don't.

Practical implication: if you're willing to assume linearity and
non-Gaussianity, you can identify direction from observational data.
This has been extended to nonlinear settings (additive noise models,
post-nonlinear models) with related identifiability results.

## Where causal discovery works, and where it doesn't

**It works**: physical systems where causal sufficiency is plausible
(gene regulatory networks with controlled experiments); large
observational studies with careful backing from domain expertise;
settings where you have partial expert knowledge to seed the
algorithm.

**It doesn't** in most economic and financial settings, because:

- Hidden confounders are essentially guaranteed (markets have
  latent factors we can't observe).
- Faithfulness is often violated (equilibrium relationships create
  cancellations by construction).
- Time-series data breaks the standard IID setup and requires
  specific extensions (Granger-causality is a related but distinct
  concept).

## What it's actually good for

Even where you can't fully trust the output, causal discovery is
useful in three ways:

- **Hypothesis generation** — surfacing candidate causal structures
  worth investigating with better data or experiments.
- **Sanity checking prior beliefs** — if your subject-matter graph is
  strongly inconsistent with the data's conditional-independence
  pattern, one of them is wrong.
- **Structuring assumptions** — running discovery forces you to make
  the sufficiency and faithfulness assumptions explicit, which is
  progress even when the conclusions are uncertain.

The lesson is the same as elsewhere in causal inference: the value
isn't necessarily a single answer, it's clarity about what you'd need
to change your mind.
