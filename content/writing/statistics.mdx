I've used statistics in two adversarial-noise settings — financial
markets and clinical data. What I actually reach for is a small subset
of what any statistics course covers. This is a note about which parts
of the curriculum earn their keep in practice.

## The three habits that matter more than any technique

**Look at the data first.** Every technique I know has been
misapplied by someone who didn't first plot the data. A histogram, a
scatter plot, and a time-series plot answer more questions than
half the tests you'd otherwise reach for. Anscombe's quartet is
overused as a warning, but the warning is real: summary statistics
lie by omission.

**Ask what would falsify the claim.** Statistics is a language for
disciplined belief updating. Any time I compute a p-value, an
effect size, or a confidence interval, I should be able to answer
"what data would make me change my mind?". If I can't, I don't
really have a statistical claim; I have a rhetorical one.

**Prefer estimation over testing.** A hypothesis test tells you
whether an effect is non-zero. An effect size with a confidence
interval tells you *how big* and *how uncertain*. In almost every
practical decision, the second is what you actually need. The
p-value ritual survives because it's a defensible default; it's
rarely the most useful answer.

## Distributions — the small set worth internalising

The heavy-hitters:

- **Gaussian.** The one everyone uses correctly and incorrectly. Its
  role in the central limit theorem is fundamental; its assumption
  as a model for individual observations is often the mistake.
- **Poisson.** For counts of independent events. The moment
  independence fails — bursty arrivals, contagion — you need
  negative binomial (over-dispersion) or a proper point-process model.
- **Log-normal.** For strictly-positive quantities whose log is
  Gaussian. Financial returns over long horizons; prices; sizes.
- **Student's t / heavy-tailed families.** For return distributions
  and any error term where extreme observations happen more often
  than a Gaussian would predict. Finance without heavy tails is
  finance with a hidden fragility.

The lesson: know which distribution matches the data-generating
process, not which one makes the math easier.

## Regression — what to remember about the workhorse

Linear regression is the most useful and most misused tool in the
practitioner's kit. Two under-appreciated points:

**Regression coefficients are conditional statements.** $\beta_j$
tells you what $Y$ does when $X_j$ changes *holding all other
covariates fixed*. Whether that's the causal effect depends on
whether the "held-fixed" covariates form a valid adjustment set. In
observational data, they rarely do without an explicit causal
argument.

**Diagnostics matter more than the fit.** Residual plots, leverage,
Cook's distance, VIF for multicollinearity. A high $R^2$ with
patterned residuals is worse than a lower $R^2$ with clean
residuals — the second at least isn't lying to you about which
model to trust.

## Hypothesis testing — the parts that are actually useful

Testing is *most* useful when the null is clean, the alternative is
specific, and the decision has a real cost. A/B testing on a website
fits this: null is "no difference in conversion rate", alternative
is a specific business hypothesis, the decision is to ship or not.

Testing is *least* useful in exploratory science, where a rejected
null tells you something is happening but not what, and where
multiple-comparison corrections take a large chunk of your power
before you get to any specific claim.

## Time series — where practitioners actually lose the most

The single largest source of practical statistical errors I've seen
is treating time-series data as if it were IID. Auto-correlation
inflates apparent significance; standard errors are wrong; ordinary
least squares standard errors on serially-correlated regressors are
biased toward false positives.

The minimum viable defence:

- **Newey–West standard errors** for time-series regression.
- **Block bootstrap** rather than IID bootstrap.
- **Walk-forward validation** rather than random-split cross-validation
  for any predictive task with a time index.
- **Explicit tests** for stationarity (ADF, KPSS) and cointegration
  (Engle–Granger, Johansen) when you're regressing one time series on
  another.

## Bayesian vs frequentist

I use both. Frequentist tools are cleaner when the sample is large,
the model is well-specified, and the question fits the null-hypothesis
framing. Bayesian tools are cleaner when I have real prior
information, when the sample is small, or when I want a full
posterior distribution to reason with rather than a point estimate
plus interval.

The distinction matters less to me in day-to-day work than the
educational literature suggests. What matters is which framing gives
me the estimate I need with the right characterisation of
uncertainty for the decision I'm about to make.
