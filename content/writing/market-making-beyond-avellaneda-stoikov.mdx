The Avellaneda–Stoikov formulas are the first thing anyone implements, and
[the derivation](/writing/avellaneda-stoikov/) is worth doing once by hand.
But they carry two defects that surface on the first day of live quoting. The
skew term $q\gamma\sigma^2(T-t)$ is linear in inventory and unbounded, so it
says nothing sensible about approaching a position limit. And everything
depends on a terminal time $T$ that a continuously quoting desk does not have.

The literature that followed — Guéant with Lehalle and Fernandez-Tapia, then
the book — repairs both. Most of that work is machinery for making the results
provable: verification theorems, admissibility, the construction of a point
process whose intensity a predictable control may legitimately alter. None of
it changes a quote. Three parts do.

## Inventory limits change what you ship

Impose $|q| \le Q$ — every desk has a cap anyway — and the infinite family of
coupled nonlinear ODEs indexed by inventory becomes a finite system of $2Q+1$
equations. The substitution $v = u^{-\gamma/k}$ then makes that system linear,

$$
u'(t) = A\,u(t),
$$

with $A$ tridiagonal: the diagonal quadratic in $q$, the off-diagonals
constant. Solve it once offline and what you ship is a table of bid and ask
quotes indexed by inventory level, not a formula evaluated per tick. That is a
different object, and it is what desks actually run.

The behaviour near the cap is also qualitatively right, which the original is
not. Approaching the limit, the correct response is to go sharply asymmetric
and eventually withdraw one side entirely at $q = \pm Q$ — not to keep adding
another $\gamma\sigma^2(T-t)$ of skew per lot indefinitely.

## Quotes without a clock

As $T-t$ grows the solution converges, and the limiting quotes are read off the
eigenvector associated with the extreme eigenvalue of $A$. The stationary
policy is an eigenvector problem, and the horizon drops out of the answer
altogether.

This matters more than it sounds. The Avellaneda–Stoikov spread collapses as
$t \to T$, which is an artefact of a problem posed with a liquidation deadline
rather than anything about market making. Remove the deadline and you have
constant quotes per inventory level: a policy you can leave running, instead of
a formula you keep patching around the end of its own horizon.

## The fill curve you actually observe

The exponential intensity $\Lambda(\delta) = Ae^{-k\delta}$ is chosen because it
makes the algebra collapse, not because order flow behaves that way.
Empirically, fill probability against distance from the mid looks closer to a
power law. This is not an academic complaint: $k$ is a parameter you estimate
from your own fills, and fitting an exponential to power-law data biases your
spread systematically in one direction.

The framework survives a general decreasing intensity subject to a regularity
condition, roughly $\Lambda\Lambda'' < 2(\Lambda')^2$, which is what guarantees
the quote maximising the Hamiltonian is unique. Power-law shapes satisfy it. So
you can calibrate to what your fills say rather than to what keeps the ODEs
tractable.

A related piece of practical relief: replacing exponential utility with a
running inventory penalty $\phi\int q^2\,dt$ yields the same asymptotic quotes
under a mapping between $\gamma$ and $\phi$. Nobody has intuition for a CARA
parameter; $\phi$ can be set against a stated tolerance for inventory.

## What the model still cannot see

Both Avellaneda–Stoikov and everything built on it assume the mid is a
martingale and that fills carry no information. Adverse selection is absent by
construction. On a real book it is where the P&L goes, so the rigour buys you a
correct answer to a model that omits the thing which hurts most.

The multi-asset extension is the exception worth the effort — quoting a curve
or a set of crosses, skewing against correlated inventory through a covariance
matrix rather than treating each book separately is the highest-value result in
this literature. It inherits the same blind spot.
