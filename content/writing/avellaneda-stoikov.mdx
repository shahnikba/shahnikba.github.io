## The state and the wager

A market maker's position at time $t$ is three numbers: cash $x$,
inventory $q$, and the mid-price $S$. Everything else — quotes, fills,
the drift of the book — is machinery for moving between such triples.
The objective is expected utility of terminal wealth marked at the mid,
so the value function is

$$
V(x,q,S,t) = \sup_{\delta^a,\delta^b}\ \mathbb{E}_t\!\left[-e^{-\gamma(X_T + q_T S_T)}\right].
$$

The exponential utility is not decoration. It is what makes the problem
tractable later, and it is also an honest statement of what a market
maker dislikes: not variance of returns as such, but ending the session
holding inventory that has moved against him.

## One instant of trading

Over an interval $dt$ only three things can happen. With probability
$\lambda^a\, dt$ the ask is lifted: cash becomes $x + S + \delta^a$ and
inventory falls to $q - 1$. With probability $\lambda^b\, dt$ the bid
is hit: cash becomes $x - S + \delta^b$ and inventory rises to
$q + 1$. Otherwise nothing trades and only the price moves. Bellman's
principle says today's value equals the expected value one instant
later, which is that three-way average weighted by those probabilities.

## From expectation to equation

Between fills the mid diffuses, $dS = \sigma\, dW$, so Itô's lemma
applied to the no-trade branch gives
$V + V_t\, dt + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 V_{SS}\, dt$ once
$\mathbb{E}[dS] = 0$ and $(dS)^2 = \sigma^2\, dt$ are used. The
execution branches admit no such expansion: inventory jumps by a whole
unit, the state moves discontinuously, and what enters is a difference
in value rather than a derivative. Cancelling $V$ and dividing by $dt$
leaves

$$
0 = V_t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 V_{SS} + \lambda^a (V_a - V) + \lambda^b (V_b - V).
$$

The maximisation enters because the intensities are ours to set:
$\lambda^a = A e^{-k \delta^a}$ and $\lambda^b = A e^{-k \delta^b}$.
Quoting tighter buys flow at the cost of edge, so

$$
0 = V_t + \tfrac{1}{2}\sigma^2 V_{SS} + \max_{\delta^a} \lambda^a (V_a - V) + \max_{\delta^b} \lambda^b (V_b - V).
$$

Each execution term reads as probability of a fill times the gain in
continuation value from landing in the post-fill state. Selling is
worth doing when it moves inventory towards where you want it, not
because a sale is intrinsically good.

## Why exponential utility collapses the problem

As written this is a PDE in cash, inventory, price and time. What
rescues it is the ansatz

$$
V(x, q, S, t) = -e^{-\gamma (x + qS)}\, v(q, t).
$$

Cash and price enter the utility only through the mark-to-market
$x + qS$, so they factor out almost entirely, leaving a system of
ordinary differential equations in $t$ indexed by integer inventory.
A further substitution $v = u^{-\gamma / k}$ linearises that system.
Four dimensions become one, plus a counter.

## What the closed form is, and what it is not

From the reduced problem, and only after an asymptotic expansion valid
for moderate inventory, come the two expressions everyone quotes: the
reservation price

$$
r = S - q \gamma \sigma^2 (T - t),
$$

and the optimal total spread

$$
\delta^a + \delta^b = \gamma \sigma^2 (T - t) + \frac{2}{\gamma}\, \ln\!\left(1 + \frac{\gamma}{k}\right).
$$

These are approximations derived from the HJB, not exact solutions of
the control problem, and the distinction matters in practice: the
skew term grows without bound in $q$, so the approximation degrades
exactly where inventory is large and the quoting decision is most
consequential.
