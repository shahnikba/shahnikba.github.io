The **generative adversarial network** — two neural networks locked in
an adversarial game — was one of the most consequential ideas in
machine learning of the last decade. It also turned out to be one of
the hardest to train reliably. Both parts of that story are worth
understanding, especially now that diffusion models have largely taken
its place at the frontier of image generation.

## The minimax formulation

A GAN has two players. A **generator** $G_\theta$ maps a noise vector
$z \sim p_Z$ to a sample in data space. A **discriminator** $D_\phi$
outputs the probability that its input is a real training example
rather than a $G$-generated fake. The players minimise / maximise
opposite sides of a common objective:

$$
\min_{\theta} \max_{\phi} \; \mathbb{E}_{x \sim p_{\text{data}}}[\log D_\phi(x)] + \mathbb{E}_{z \sim p_Z}[\log(1 - D_\phi(G_\theta(z)))].
$$

At the theoretical equilibrium, $G$ generates samples indistinguishable
from real data and $D$ can only guess. In practice you never reach
equilibrium — you approach it via alternating gradient updates.

## Why they're hard to train

Three failure modes surface constantly:

**Mode collapse.** The generator finds one plausible output that
fools the discriminator and produces it (or slight variations)
regardless of the input noise. The samples look real but the
diversity of the true data is lost. This is the classic GAN
frustration: a beautiful cat that's always the same cat.

**Discriminator saturation.** If $D$ becomes too good too fast, its
gradient with respect to $G$-outputs shrinks towards zero — the log-
sigmoid saturates when the fake is confidently rejected. $G$
then has no useful signal to learn from. The community's response
was to keep $D$ deliberately weak — carefully limiting its capacity
or training rate.

**Non-stationary target.** Both $G$ and $D$ are moving. Standard
optimisation theory doesn't apply cleanly when the objective is
adversarial. Two-player games generically don't have simple
convergence guarantees; equilibria may not exist, may not be unique,
may not be reachable via gradient descent.

## The workarounds

An enormous body of research produced fixes and variants. A few of
the most useful:

- **Wasserstein GAN** (WGAN) replaced the log-loss with the
  Earth-Mover distance, whose gradient doesn't vanish even when $D$
  perfectly distinguishes the two distributions. This alone made
  training substantially more stable. WGAN-GP adds a gradient penalty
  to enforce the Lipschitz condition, giving the still-most-widely-
  used formulation.
- **Spectral normalisation** on discriminator weights bounds the
  Lipschitz constant differently; sometimes complementary to WGAN-GP.
- **Progressive growing** (Karras et al., ProGAN, then StyleGAN)
  starts training on low-resolution images and progressively adds
  detail. Produced the first genuinely photorealistic face generators.
- **Careful architectural choices** — no batch norm in $D$, spectral
  normalisation, TTUR (two-time-scale updates giving $D$ and $G$
  different learning rates).

## Why diffusion models took over

Around 2020–2022, denoising diffusion models overtook GANs on almost
every benchmark. Three reasons:

- **Stable training.** Diffusion models are trained with a simple
  regression objective (predict the noise added to a corrupted
  sample). No minimax, no adversarial dynamics. You get sensible
  results with default hyperparameters — a bar GANs almost never met.
- **Mode coverage.** Diffusion training is likelihood-adjacent, which
  strongly discourages the mode-collapse failure mode.
- **Scale.** Diffusion models scale up cleanly with more parameters
  and more compute; GAN scaling was always more delicate.

At the time of writing, essentially all frontier image-generation
work uses diffusion (Stable Diffusion, DALL·E, Midjourney) or
diffusion-adjacent methods. GANs still have niches — real-time
generation (they're fast at inference), image-to-image translation
(pix2pix, CycleGAN) — but the frontier has moved on.

## What GANs got right

Even in retirement, GANs left a durable contribution. The adversarial
training idea shows up in domain adaptation, robust training, and
some diffusion refinements. The lesson that a network can learn a
distribution *implicitly* — via samples without a tractable density
— shaped how everyone thinks about generative models. And StyleGAN's
disentangled latent spaces remain the reference for controllable
image synthesis.

The broader lesson from watching GANs rise and fall: **training
stability compounds**. A method that works reliably out of the box
gets iterated on faster; a fragile method gets abandoned even if its
best cases are impressive.
