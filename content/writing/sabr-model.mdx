## 1. Model dynamics

The SABR (Stochastic Alpha Beta Rho) model assumes that the forward
price $F_t$ and its instantaneous volatility $\alpha_t$ evolve
according to

$$
dF_t = \alpha_t F_t^{\beta} \, dW_1,
$$

$$
d\alpha_t = \nu \alpha_t \, dW_2,
$$

with

$$
dW_1 \, dW_2 = \rho \, dt,
$$

where:

- $F_t$: forward price
- $\alpha_t$: instantaneous volatility
- $\beta$: elasticity parameter
- $\nu$: volatility of volatility (vol-of-vol)
- $\rho$: correlation between the forward and volatility

---

## 2. Intuition behind the parameters

The SABR parameters can be viewed as controlling four independent
characteristics of the implied-volatility surface:

| Parameter | Primary effect |
| --- | --- |
| **α (Alpha)** | Volatility level |
| **β (Beta)** | Backbone / elasticity |
| **ρ (Rho)** | Skew |
| **ν (Nu)** | Smile curvature (convexity) |

---

## 3. α (Alpha) — overall volatility level

Alpha determines the overall height of the volatility surface. A
larger $\alpha$ raises implied volatility almost uniformly across
strikes.

Near the money,

$$
\sigma_{\text{ATM}} \approx \frac{\alpha}{F^{1-\beta}}.
$$

Therefore $\alpha$ primarily controls:

- ATM volatility
- Overall volatility level
- Vertical shift of the smile

**Trader intuition:**

> *"The whole surface is too low (or too high)."* Adjust **α**.

---

## 4. β (Beta) — backbone

Beta controls how volatility changes as the forward price moves.
The instantaneous volatility is

$$
\alpha F^{\beta}.
$$

Different values correspond to different assumptions.

### β = 1

Lognormal dynamics (Black model):

- Percentage moves are constant.
- Standard Black-76 assumption.
- Common in FX.

### β = 0

Normal dynamics (Bachelier model):

- Absolute moves are constant.
- Frequently used when rates are low or may become negative.

### 0 < β < 1

Intermediate behaviour. This determines the **backbone** — how the
ATM volatility changes when the forward level changes.

**Trader intuition:**

$\beta$ does **not** mainly change the smile shape. Instead, it
determines how the entire smile moves as the underlying forward
changes.

Typical values:

- Swaptions: 0 or 0.5
- Caps / Floors: 0.5
- FX: 1
- Commodities: 0.5 – 1

---

## 5. ρ (Rho) — skew

$\rho$ is the correlation between forward moves and volatility moves.
It controls the asymmetry of the implied-volatility smile.

**Negative $\rho$:**

- Forward decreases
- Volatility increases
- Left wing becomes higher

**Positive $\rho$:**

- Forward increases
- Volatility increases
- Right wing becomes higher

Thus $\rho$ mainly controls:

- Direction of the skew
- Magnitude of the skew

**Trader intuition:**

> *"The left wing is too high."* Decrease $\rho$ (make it more
> negative).

---

## 6. ν (Nu) — volatility of volatility

Nu governs the stochasticity of $\alpha$:

$$
d\alpha = \nu \alpha \, dW.
$$

If $\nu = 0$, volatility becomes deterministic and the smile almost
disappears. Larger $\nu$ produces:

- stronger smile
- more curvature
- fatter wings

It mainly controls smile convexity and smile intensity.

**Trader intuition:**

> *"The smile is too flat."* Increase $\nu$.

---

## 7. Interaction between parameters

Although traders often summarise the parameters as *"ρ controls the
skew, ν controls the smile"*, they are not completely independent.

In Hagan's implied-volatility approximation, one correction term is

$$
\frac{\rho \beta \nu \alpha}{4 (FK)^{(1-\beta)/2}}.
$$

Consequently:

- Increasing $\nu$ also amplifies the skew.
- $\rho$ determines the **direction** of the skew.
- $\nu$ determines the **strength** (curvature) of the smile and
  also reinforces the skew.
- $\beta$ determines how the smile evolves as the forward moves.
- $\alpha$ anchors the overall volatility level.

Experienced traders frequently adjust **ρ and ν together** during
calibration.

---

## 8. Why β is usually fixed

In practice, most trading desks do **not** calibrate $\beta$.
Instead, $\beta$ is chosen in advance (for example 0, 0.5, or 1).

The reason: $\alpha$ and $\beta$ are highly correlated. Changing
$\beta$ can often be compensated by changing $\alpha$, making
calibration unstable and producing non-unique solutions.

Typical calibration therefore estimates only $\alpha$, $\rho$, and
$\nu$ while $\beta$ remains fixed.

---

## 9. Practical calibration workflow

A trader looking at a market volatility smile typically thinks as
follows:

| Market observation | Parameter to adjust |
| --- | --- |
| Entire surface too low / high | Increase / decrease $\alpha$ |
| Smile too flat | Increase $\nu$ |
| Smile too curved | Decrease $\nu$ |
| Left wing too high | Make $\rho$ more negative |
| Right wing too high | Make $\rho$ more positive |
| ATM volatility changes incorrectly as forwards move | Adjust $\beta$ |

## 10. Trader's cheat sheet

| Parameter | Intuition | Main effect |
| --- | --- | --- |
| **α (Alpha)** | Overall volatility level | Vertical shift of the smile |
| **β (Beta)** | Elasticity / backbone | How ATM volatility evolves as the forward moves |
| **ρ (Rho)** | Spot–volatility correlation | Skew (left vs. right wing) |
| **ν (Nu)** | Volatility of volatility | Smile curvature (convexity) |

---

## 11. Key takeaways

- $\alpha$ sets the overall level of implied volatility.
- $\beta$ controls the backbone of the volatility surface and is
  usually fixed.
- $\rho$ determines the sign and magnitude of the skew.
- $\nu$ controls the curvature (convexity) of the smile and also
  amplifies skew.
- The combination of these four parameters allows SABR to fit market
  volatility smiles while remaining computationally efficient, which
  explains its widespread use on interest-rate, FX, and commodity
  option desks.
