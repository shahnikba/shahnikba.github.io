Hagan's SABR model — Stochastic Alpha Beta Rho — is one of those
places where the parametric structure survives being wrong about the
world because the parameters map cleanly onto how a trader thinks
about the volatility surface. Four numbers, four intuitions, and the
whole surface can be reshaped one lever at a time.

The dynamics are the usual two-factor SDE: the forward price $F_t$
and its instantaneous volatility $\alpha_t$ each follow a driftless
diffusion, with the two Brownian motions correlated by $\rho$:

$$
dF_t = \alpha_t F_t^{\beta} \, dW_1, \qquad
d\alpha_t = \nu \alpha_t \, dW_2, \qquad
dW_1 \, dW_2 = \rho \, dt.
$$

Four parameters do all the work.

## What each parameter does to the smile

**$\alpha$ sets the height.** Near the money, the implied volatility
is roughly $\alpha / F^{1-\beta}$, so $\alpha$ moves the whole surface
up and down more or less uniformly. If the entire surface is too low
or too high, $\alpha$ is the lever.

**$\beta$ is the backbone.** It doesn't primarily reshape the smile;
it decides how the smile *translates* as the underlying forward
moves. $\beta = 1$ recovers Black lognormal dynamics — the FX
convention. $\beta = 0$ recovers Bachelier normal dynamics, which is
what rates desks tend to use whenever the underlying can plausibly go
negative. Anything in between (typically 0.5 for swaptions,
caps/floors, and commodities) is a compromise between the two.

**$\rho$ is the skew.** As the correlation between forward moves and
volatility moves, $\rho$ decides which wing of the smile is higher.
Negative $\rho$ raises the left wing (equity-index style: down moves
come with volatility spikes). Positive $\rho$ raises the right wing.
It controls both the direction and the magnitude of the skew.

**$\nu$ is the smile curvature.** The vol-of-vol. If $\nu = 0$ the
volatility is deterministic and the smile essentially disappears; the
larger $\nu$, the more convex the smile and the fatter the wings.

The clean mental model is: $\alpha$ moves the surface vertically,
$\beta$ handles how it moves with the underlying, $\rho$ tilts it,
$\nu$ bends it. That mapping is what makes the model teachable.

## Where the clean mapping breaks

The parameters aren't strictly orthogonal — Hagan's implied-vol
approximation contains a correction term

$$
\frac{\rho \beta \nu \alpha}{4 (FK)^{(1-\beta)/2}}
$$

that couples them. In particular, increasing $\nu$ also *amplifies*
whatever skew $\rho$ has already introduced, so a smile that's too
tilted *and* too flat can't be fixed by moving $\rho$ alone. Any
experienced hand ends up nudging $\rho$ and $\nu$ together at
calibration time, not one after the other.

## Why $\beta$ is usually fixed before calibration

$\alpha$ and $\beta$ are highly correlated — you can compensate a
change in $\beta$ by re-fitting $\alpha$, and the calibrator will
gladly find non-unique solutions in the joint space. The practical
convention is to pick $\beta$ in advance (0 or 0.5 for rates, 1 for
FX, 0.5–1 for commodities) based on the desk's view of the
underlying's dynamics, then calibrate only $\alpha$, $\rho$, $\nu$
against the market.

## The workflow on the desk

At the market surface, most days there are only four moves worth
knowing:

- The whole surface is too low or too high → adjust $\alpha$.
- The smile is too flat or too curved → adjust $\nu$.
- One wing is systematically off → adjust $\rho$ in the wing's
  direction.
- The ATM vol drifts oddly as the forward moves — the backbone
  isn't right → the assumed $\beta$ deserves a look.

Four levers, four questions, one surface. That efficiency — plus
Hagan's closed-form implied-volatility approximation — is why SABR
is still the default on interest-rate, FX, and commodity option
desks two decades after it appeared, despite being wrong about
plenty of things.
