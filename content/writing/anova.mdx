I prefer to simulate the data. It removes any doubt about whether the
statistical machinery is doing what you think it is.

## Data simulation

Take a plant-growth experiment with two factors: **sunlight level**
(none, low, medium, high) and **watering frequency** (weekly, daily).
We generate 800 observations — 100 per (sunlight × watering) cell —
with a real interaction effect between the two factors.

The story we want the data to tell: watering only matters if there's
enough sunlight. Under low light nothing grows regardless of water;
under high light, daily watering pulls ahead of weekly.

## Fitting the model

We fit a two-way ANOVA with interaction. The main effects for
sunlight and watering both come out significant, and the
interaction term is also significant — which we expected, because
we baked it in.

The model explains about **58.8% of the variance** in plant growth
(adjusted $R^2$). Not bad for a two-factor design.

## Diagnostics

Two checks before we trust the model.

**Normality of residuals.** A Q-Q plot of residuals against a
theoretical normal shows the points falling reasonably close to the
diagonal. No egregious tail issues.

**Homogeneity of variance.** Levene's test on the cells returns
$p = 0.616$ — well above any conventional cutoff — so we cannot reject
the null hypothesis of equal variances across groups. Good.

## Pairwise comparisons

The interaction is significant, so a global $F$-test isn't enough:
we want to know **which** sunlight-watering combinations differ
from which. Tukey's HSD does this while controlling family-wise
error.

The comparisons show significant differences across most sunlight
levels *within* each watering group — the interesting places to look
if you're deciding how much to water at a given light level.

## Visualising it

Boxplots grouped by (sunlight, watering) with the pairwise
$p$-values overlaid tell the whole story on one figure. Growth rises
with sunlight in both watering conditions; the daily-watering
condition pulls further ahead as sunlight increases; and the
significant pairs are exactly where the boxplots visibly separate.

If a reader wants to reproduce it, the code is on GitHub and takes
about thirty lines of R.
