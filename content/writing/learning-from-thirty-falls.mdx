## Thirty events

A monitored elderly cohort might yield ten thousand patient-days of continuous
accelerometry and, in all of it, perhaps thirty genuine falls. At window level
the positive rate sits somewhere around $10^{-6}$, and three consequences
follow immediately. Any classifier reaches 99.99% accuracy by predicting "no
fall" forever. The model is determined by a few dozen examples, so variance
dominates bias entirely. And cross-validation stops being informative: a
five-fold split leaves six events per test fold, and the confidence interval on
sensitivity spans most of the unit interval.

The standard imbalance remedies do not survive contact with this. SMOTE
interpolates linearly between neighbouring minority points in feature space,
which for a time series is meaningless — the average of two falls is not a
fall. It is a smeared waveform occupying a region of signal space no real body
ever visits, and a classifier will learn to separate it from nothing at all,
then report that it has learned something.

## Symmetries you can prove

Before reaching for anything adversarial there is a deterministic toolkit for
inertial data, catalogued by Um and colleagues in their work on Parkinson's
monitoring: jitter for sensor noise, scaling for amplitude variation between
subjects, permutation of sub-sequences, time warping for movement speed,
magnitude warping for non-uniform intensity, and rotation for device
orientation.

Rotation is the one that matters for phone-based sensing. Orientation in a
pocket or on a wrist is genuinely arbitrary and the label is provably invariant
to it, which is exactly the property a good augmentation needs: it should
encode a symmetry the physics is known to have, not one you hope it has. Um et
al. found rotation combined with permutation gave the largest gain. These
methods are cheap, interpretable, and generally capture most of the benefit
available.

## What a generator does not know

Deterministic transforms move you only along symmetry directions you have
already thought of. They cannot invent a new kind of fall — backwards onto
carpet when every observed event was forwards onto tile. A conditional
generative model, in principle, learns the manifold and reaches modes you never
sampled. The architectures worth trying are conditional GANs on raw windows,
the recurrent conditional GAN of Esteban and colleagues, built for medical time
series specifically, and DCGANs on spectrogram representations, which converts
the problem into an image one where the machinery is far more mature.

There is a circularity here that is easy to miss. To train a generator that
produces falls you need enough falls to train the generator. With thirty events
you cannot. The regime where this works is a few hundred to a few thousand
positives — moderate scarcity, not the extreme case — and below that the
technique simply is not available.

The deeper problem is that a real fall has physics behind it. Acceleration
magnitude collapses towards zero for three to six hundred milliseconds of
near-freefall, then a sharp impact spike of several g, then near-zero variance
for seconds or minutes, and the gravity vector ends up pointing somewhere it
was not. A GAN optimises for indistinguishability under a learned discriminator
that has no notion of any of this, and will cheerfully emit a trace that is
spectrally plausible and physically impossible: an impact with no preceding
freefall, a gravity vector that does not integrate consistently. Check the
generated samples against those constraints explicitly, as a post-hoc filter or
an auxiliary loss. Plotting a hundred synthetic traces beside real ones is
worth more than any aggregate similarity score.

## Measuring whether it worked

Split by subject, never by window. Gait is a biometric; windows from one person
leak across a random split and inflate everything downstream. Synthetic data
enters the training fold and nothing else — obvious, and violated constantly,
because the augmentation step usually sits before the split in the pipeline.

Then train on synthetic and test on real. Fit a classifier purely on generated
data, evaluate it on held-out real data, and compare against the
real-train/real-test baseline. If the two approach each other the generator has
captured something, and this is a far harder and more honest test than any
distributional distance.

Report the metrics that reflect what deployment costs. Not accuracy, not
ROC-AUC, but precision-recall AUC and, above all, false alarms per
patient-month. A detector with 95% sensitivity and one false alarm per user per
day is unshippable; carers stop responding inside a week.

## The unglamorous answer

For falls specifically the highest-value approach is not generative at all.
Recruit young volunteers, put down crash mats, run a scripted protocol across
fall types, collect thousands of labelled events. Then treat the gap between
simulated falls and real elderly falls as a domain adaptation problem. That is
still adversarial machinery, but pointed at the transfer gap rather than at
synthesis, and everything it operates on came out of a real body.

The other direction worth watching is pretraining on the full unlabelled
corpus, which is abundant in a way the positives never will be, and fine-tuning
on the scarce events. Generative augmentation belongs in the moderate-data
regime, validated by train-on-synthetic — and it should have to beat the
rotation baseline before anything ships.
