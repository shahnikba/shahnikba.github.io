Charles Goodhart's original 1975 formulation was about monetary
policy but the general form has become a management platitude:

> *When a measure becomes a target, it ceases to be a good measure.*

Every KPI dashboard I've ever seen ran into this failure mode. The
metric that was chosen because it correlated with what actually
mattered gets optimised, the correlation weakens, and eventually the
metric is up while the underlying reality is down. This is not a
theoretical concern; it happens in every organisation that measures
things.

## The mechanism

The failure has a specific structure:

1. Something matters (customer satisfaction, quality of a trading
   strategy, health of a codebase) but is hard to measure.
2. A proxy is chosen — NPS score, Sharpe ratio, test coverage — because
   it *correlates* with the true thing under normal conditions.
3. The proxy becomes a target: performance reviews, bonuses,
   dashboards all track it.
4. People optimise the proxy. The proxy responds — it goes up. The
   underlying thing does not, because the correlation between proxy
   and reality was *conditional* on people not gaming the proxy.

The mistake is subtle. The proxy was a good measure *of a passive
observation* of the system. It was never guaranteed to be a good
target for an actively optimising agent.

## Three signatures of a KPI going bad

**Correlation breakdown.** The KPI diverges from any independent
measure of the underlying reality. NPS is up, but retention is
flat. Test coverage is 90%, but production bugs are unchanged.
Sharpe ratio looks better, but max drawdown is worse.

**Growing effort per unit of KPI improvement.** The organisation
spends more and more resources chasing smaller improvements. This is
the signal that people are exhausting the *gameable* part of the
metric and now have to bend the underlying reality.

**Suspicious composition changes.** Investigate any large improvement
that came from a mix shift — losing "unhealthy" customers, changing
what counts as a "test", classifying certain trades differently. A
KPI improvement should be dominated by the KPI actually changing
for a stable definition, not the definition changing.

## Design principles that help

None of these are silver bullets. All of them buy time.

**Track a suite, not a single KPI.** Any one metric is gameable in
isolation. A pair of counterbalancing metrics (throughput +
quality; revenue + customer complaints; strategy PnL + max drawdown)
is harder to game because improving one degrades the other. This is
why financial trading uses risk-adjusted return rather than raw
return — the second is trivially gamed by adding leverage.

**Rotate what you look at.** People will optimise whatever's on the
dashboard. If the dashboard has too many things or changes them
periodically, it's harder to build a persistent optimisation strategy
around any single one.

**Prefer leading indicators only for diagnosis, not for compensation.**
Leading indicators (a proxy for a lagging outcome) are unusually
gameable because the lag hides the disconnect. Compensating people on
a leading indicator is the fastest way to turn it into pure noise.

**Directly measure the thing you actually care about, even when it's
expensive.** If customer satisfaction is what matters, do the survey.
If code quality matters, review the code. Proxies are for when you
truly cannot measure the real thing — not for when measuring it is
merely inconvenient.

## In trading specifically

The archetypal KPI-goes-wrong story in systematic trading is Sharpe
ratio worship. A strategy with a headline Sharpe of 2.0 sounds
strictly better than one at 1.5. But:

- Sharpe ratios of 2.0+ on strategies with capacity constraints often
  reflect *sample-size winner's curse* — the top of the ranked list
  of backtested strategies. Overfit strategies live at the top.
- Sharpe punishes upside volatility as much as downside — the
  Sortino ratio is closer to what most people mean when they say
  "risk-adjusted return".
- A strategy that gambles for consistency (adds hidden tail risk) can
  raise Sharpe by lowering realised volatility, at the cost of an
  eventual blow-up that Sharpe by definition can't warn about.

Every institutional trading desk knows this. Most retail-facing
brokerages still lead with Sharpe. Draw your own conclusion about
which population is doing the optimising.

## The honest take

The right answer isn't "abandon KPIs" — measurement is essential and
you can't scale an organisation without shared metrics. The right
answer is: **respect that any KPI you deploy will be optimised, and
budget for either replacing it or auditing it before that optimisation
has time to hollow it out**. This is what changes when you take
Goodhart seriously.
