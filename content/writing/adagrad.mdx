The most straightforward improvement over plain SGD is **AdaGrad**.
The idea is small and elegant: scale each parameter's step by the
inverse-square-root of the sum of its historical squared gradients.

For parameter $\theta_i$ at step $t$, the update is

$$
G_{t,i} = \sum_{k=1}^{t} g_{k,i}^{2},
\qquad
\theta_{t+1,i} = \theta_{t,i} - \frac{\eta}{\sqrt{G_{t,i} + \varepsilon}}\; g_{t,i}.
$$

Parameters that see large gradients early get shrunk. Sparse or
rarely-updated parameters keep their step size. This is exactly right
for sparse convex problems — famously NLP with sparse features. The
first time you use AdaGrad on a bag-of-words classifier and it
converges 10× faster than tuned SGD is a compelling introduction.

## Why it eventually breaks

The problem is right there in the formula: $G_{t,i}$ is
**monotonically increasing**. Effective learning rates decay towards
zero as training progresses, even when the loss surface would benefit
from moving faster later.

On sparse convex problems this is fine — you want small steps
eventually anyway, and the sparsity means most parameters don't
accumulate much history. On dense non-convex problems (any real
neural network) it kills learning long before convergence. You watch
the training loss plateau at a bad value while the effective step
size goes to zero.

The failure is deterministic and predictable — AdaGrad will always
fail on long-horizon dense training. It's not a random-seed problem;
it's the algorithm doing what the maths says it does.

## The fix — an exponential moving average

**RMSProp** (Hinton, unpublished lecture notes) replaces the
cumulative sum with an exponentially-weighted moving average:

$$
E[g^2]_{t,i} = \beta \cdot E[g^2]_{t-1,i} + (1 - \beta) \cdot g_{t,i}^{2}
$$

with typical $\beta = 0.9$ or $0.99$. The step becomes

$$
\theta_{t+1,i} = \theta_{t,i} - \frac{\eta}{\sqrt{E[g^2]_{t,i} + \varepsilon}}\; g_{t,i}.
$$

Same per-parameter adaptivity as AdaGrad; the moving average forgets
old gradients so the effective learning rate doesn't decay to zero.
Simple, and it worked well enough that it's often the default
optimiser in RL and RNN training even now.
