The most straightforward improvement over plain SGD is AdaGrad.
The idea is small and elegant: **scale each parameter's step by the
inverse-square-root of the sum of its historical squared gradients**.

For parameter $\theta_i$ at step $t$, the update is

$$
G_{t,i} = \sum_{k=1}^{t} g_{k,i}^{2},
\qquad
\theta_{t+1,i} = \theta_{t,i} - \frac{\eta}{\sqrt{G_{t,i} + \varepsilon}}\; g_{t,i}.
$$

Parameters that see large gradients early get shrunk; sparse or
rarely-updated parameters keep their step size. This works
beautifully on sparse convex problems — famously NLP with sparse
features.

## Why it eventually breaks

The problem is right there in the formula: $G_{t,i}$ is
**monotonically increasing**. Effective learning rates decay
towards zero as training progresses, even when the loss surface
would benefit from moving faster later. On non-convex problems with
long training horizons — which is basically everything today — this
kills learning long before you converge.

RMSProp and Adam patch this by using an *exponentially-weighted*
moving average instead of a cumulative sum. Same idea, without the
death spiral.
